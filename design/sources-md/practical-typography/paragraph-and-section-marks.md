paragraph and section marksInsert a nonbreaking space

The paragraph mark (¶) is used when citing documents with sequentially numbered paragraphs. The section mark (§) is used when citing documents with numbered or lettered sections.

|     |                | Windows  | Mac OS     | HTML     |
|-----|----------------|----------|------------|----------|
| ¶   | paragraph mark | alt 0182 | option + 7 | `&para;` |
| §   | section mark   | alt 0167 | option + 6 | `&sect;` |

The paragraph mark is also known as a pilcrow.

A paragraph mark or section mark should always be followed by a <a href="nonbreaking-spaces.html" class="xref">nonbreaking space</a>. The nonbreaking space acts like glue that keeps the mark joined with the numeric reference that <span class="no-hyphens">follows.</span>

Without the nonbreaking space, the mark and the reference can end up on separate lines or pages. This can confuse <span class="no-hyphens">readers.</span>

If the paragraph or section reference comes at the start of a sentence, don’t use the mark—spell out the whole word (Section 17200 applied to the transaction, but § 17500 did not). In a reference to multiple paragraphs or sections, double the mark (¶¶ or §§).

Though these marks are used most frequently by legal and academic writers, there’s no reason others shouldn’t use them (See § 31.4.2 of the manual). Why not? They look <span class="no-hyphens">cool.</span>

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- Occasionally you see paragraph marks used within a solid block of text to denote internal paragraphs. This is archaic. If you need to denote real paragraphs (not references to paragraphs) then use a <a href="first-line-indents.html" class="xref">first-line indent</a> or <a href="space-between-paragraphs.html" class="xref">space between paragraphs</a>.
