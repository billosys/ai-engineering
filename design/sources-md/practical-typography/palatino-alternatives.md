Palatino is the work of Hermann Zapf, a calligrapher by training. Many of his fonts reflect this influence. But the Palatino <a href="system-fonts.html" class="xref">system font</a> is a harsh representation of Zapf’s original design. <a href="fontrec/palatino-nova.html" class="xref">Palatino Nova</a> is Zapf’s own reworking of Palatino that restores its original <span class="no-hyphens">subtlety.</span>

Despite the name, <a href="fontrec/iowan-old-style.html" class="xref">Iowan Old Style</a> is also based on Italian Renaissance typography, though more loosely. The lovely <a href="fontrec/verdigris.html" class="xref">Verdigris</a> also draws on these <span class="no-hyphens">influences.</span>

I’ve always liked Palatino a lot. But the decades of abuse and misuse have dimmed the novelty it once had. I designed as a hybrid of [Aldus,](http://www.rightreading.com/typehead/aldus.htm) a book face that Zapf derived from Palatino, and Trump Mediaeval, a text face by Zapf’s contemporary [Georg Trump,](http://luc.devroye.org/fonts-26218.html) another fantastic calligrapher-turned-type <span class="no-hyphens">designer.</span>

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- These alternatives apply equally to Book Antiqua, which was created as a Palatino clone for Microsoft Windows. Book Antiqua has caused its share of controversy over the years—Zapf himself considered it an unethical pillaging of his work. But in recent times, the alleged pillager (Monotype) acquired the alleged pillagee (Linotype), thereby extinguishing the <span class="no-hyphens">beef.</span>

- Fans of Palatino or Zapf will enjoy Robert Bringhurst’s excellent book [*Palatino: The Natural History of a Typeface,*](http://www.godine.com/book/palatino/) which catalogs the many variations of Palatino that Zapf made over 60 years, and gives due credit to his punchcutter August <span class="no-hyphens">Rosenberger.</span>
