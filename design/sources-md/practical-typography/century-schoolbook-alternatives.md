The “Scotch Roman” style of text face has been consistently popular since the mid-1800s, and traces its roots to [the Edinburgh foundry](http://typefoundry.blogspot.co.nz/2007/02/scotch-roman.html) of William Miller. The original font named Century, designed by Linn Boyd Benton in 1894, was derived from this Scotch model. Since then, the Scotch flavor has lived on in many other faces, some carrying “Century” in their name, others not. (For instance, the [Georgia](georgia-alternatives.md) <a href="system-fonts.html" class="xref">system font</a> is also in the Scotch <span class="no-hyphens">family.)</span>

I include the Century Schoolbook system font on my list of “generally tolerable” <a href="system-fonts.html" class="xref">system fonts</a>. But it’s hardly the nicest Century. Rather, it’s a later spinoff created by Morris Fuller Benton (Linn’s son) from research about what children found easy to read—hence the name, which contemplates its intended use. It’s not bad. But its letterforms are rather loose and <span class="no-hyphens">broad.</span>

The Scotch category is full of better options. A few of my favorites are <a href="fontrec/miller.html" class="xref">Miller</a> (named after the Edinburgh foundry), <a href="fontrec/harriet.html" class="xref">Harriet</a>, and <a href="fontrec/ingeborg.html" class="xref">Ingeborg</a>. I’ve also designed my own take on this style called , which brings together things I like about a number of Scotch-style faces from the early- to <span class="no-hyphens">mid-1900s.</span>

- Century Schoolbook is used in the PDF opinions of the United States Supreme Court, and countless law-school textbooks as <span class="no-hyphens">well.</span>
