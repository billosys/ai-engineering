Identifying fonts

I recommended in <a href="maxims-of-page-layout.html" class="xref">maxims of page layout</a> that you imitate typography you like, including font choices. But how do you identify a font just by looking at <span class="no-hyphens">it?</span>

The traditional way is to practice. After more than 20 years of studying fonts, I estimate that I can identify about 300 distinct families by sight. Compared to the more than 100,000 digital fonts available, that’s not many. But it covers about 95% of the fonts used out in the world. So when I see a font, I usually know what it <span class="no-hyphens">is.</span>

You, however, are not at that point. Therefore, some tips to improve your font-spotting <span class="no-hyphens">skills.</span>

1.  **In books, seek out colophons.**  
    A colophon is a note describing the printing and typography of the book. Usually the colophon appears in the endpapers. Sometimes you’ll find them squeezed into the copyright page at the front. A basic colophon will identify the fonts used. Better ones will also credit the designers and explain why the fonts were chosen. This book has a colophon (see the <a href="#" class="xref">end credits</a>) but it’s not strictly necessary because <span class="no-hyphens">… </span>

2.  **On websites, read the CSS.**  
    Web jocks can use the HTML/CSS source inspector in any web browser to find out which fonts are being used. (Hint: look for the `font-family` <span class="no-hyphens">property.)</span>

    The lazy yet curious might enjoy the [WhatFont extension](https://chrome.google.com/webstore/detail/whatfont/jabopobgcpjmedljpbcaablpmlmfcogm?hl=en) for Chrome, which reveals the font of any text on a web page—just point <span class="no-hyphens">at it.</span>

3.  **For other printed matter, try [WhatTheFont.](http://www.myfonts.com/WhatTheFont/)**  
    WhatTheFont—no relation to WhatFont, above—is a clever service that lets you upload a photo of type and mark the characters therein. With those clues, WhatTheFont tries to identify the font algorithmically. If you have a good photo, and the font is distinctive, it works pretty <span class="no-hyphens">well.</span>

4.  **Try [Identifont.](http://www.identifont.com/)**  
    Identifont is like Wikipedia for fonts and font designers. It includes several tools for identifying fonts from their visible <span class="no-hyphens">characteristics.</span>

5.  **Ask a typographer.**  
    The easiest way is to take a picture and tweet it toward [@Font_ID](https://twitter.com/font_id) or [@TypeID,](https://twitter.com/typeid) who are both hard to <span class="no-hyphens">stump.</span>

6.  **Study key characters.**  
    Identifying fonts becomes easier as you learn what makes them distinct. Some of this will come naturally with using them. But every font has a few characters with unusual details. If you notice and remember those details, you’ll be able to identify more fonts. For example, it’s very hard to identify fonts strictly by their punctuation characters, which tend to be more alike than different. But characters like A, E, G, M, Q, R, S, a, e, f, g, r, s, t—and the <a href="ampersands.html" class="xref">ampersand</a>, of course—usually have easy-to-recognize <span class="no-hyphens">differences.</span>
