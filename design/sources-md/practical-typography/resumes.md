résumésAvoid density with a second page

As a law student, I went on a few job interviews. At one, the interviewer’s first comment was “It’s so unusual that I see a résumé without any <span class="no-hyphens">typos.”</span>

“Are you serious?” I <span class="no-hyphens">said.</span>

“Yes,” she said, “probably 90% of the résumés I get have typos. And that includes the ones we get from the top <span class="no-hyphens">schools.”</span>

Résumé is the original spelling and still preferred. Resumé is acceptable. Resume is common but <span class="no-hyphens">wrong.</span>

I got the job. There were surely better-qualified candidates. But they damaged their chances with sloppy résumés. The irony is that those people, who most needed to hear the interviewer’s feedback, weren’t in the room. Because they never got an <span class="no-hyphens">interview.</span>

Consider yourself <span class="no-hyphens">warned.</span>

This is a book on typography, not typos. But the point is the same—faced with a stack of nearly identical résumés and limited time, readers will make judgments that aren’t based on substance. Whether you think that’s fair is irrelevant. It happens all the <span class="no-hyphens">time.</span>

Students, this tip doesn’t apply to you. You’re only likely to get one page of attention, so edit <span class="no-hyphens">accordingly.</span>

The biggest problem I see with résumés is that they’re uncomfortably dense with text. I take this to be the influence of the myth that a résumé can only be one page long. Unless a potential employer demands one page, feel free to make your résumé longer, if necessary. This will ease your typographic <span class="no-hyphens">problems.</span>

Three caveats, <span class="no-hyphens">however:</span>

1.  Never assume a reader will get past the first page. Always put the most important information on page <span class="no-hyphens">one.</span>

2.  When I say “longer, if necessary”, remember you’re writing for a potential employer, not your mom. My résumé fits on two pages. I’ll bet yours can <span class="no-hyphens">too.</span>

3.  Students, this advice doesn’t apply to you. You’ve only got one page of material. <span class="no-hyphens">Really.</span>

</div>

Print a two-page résumé with two sheets of paper, even if you have a duplex printer. You don’t want a potential employer to overlook the second page because it’s printed on the back of the <span class="no-hyphens">first.</span>

1.  <a href="page-margins.html" class="xref">Page margins</a> too small; <a href="line-length.html" class="xref">line length</a> too <span class="no-hyphens">big.</span>

2.  <a href="body-text.html" class="xref">Body text</a> set in a <a href="system-fonts.html" class="xref">system font</a> <span class="no-hyphens">(Calibri).</span>

3.  Headings and gray boxes are too large relative to body <span class="no-hyphens">text.</span>

4.  Key information—the where and when—is <span class="no-hyphens">buried.</span>

5.  Ugly <a href="bulleted-and-numbered-lists.html" class="xref">bulleted lists</a>.

<a href="samples/resume-after.pdf" class="sample-document"></a>

<div id="after" class="subhead">

[After](#after)

</div>

1.  Page margins bigger; line length <span class="no-hyphens">smaller.</span>

2.  Calibri replaced with and .

3.  Headings no longer dominate the <span class="no-hyphens">page.</span>

4.  Names of schools and employers are easily <span class="no-hyphens">noticed.</span>

5.  Gentler list <span class="no-hyphens">bullets.</span>

6.  Nonessential information moved to second <span class="no-hyphens">page.</span>

Keep in mind that one way a résumé illustrates your virtues is by drawing connections between you—whom the reader knows nothing about—and various schools and employers, which the reader may have heard of. The implied syllogism goes like this: *Boxer Bedley & Ball is an elite employer. This person worked at Boxer Bedley & Ball. Therefore, this person is an elite-quality candidate.* Don’t make your reader struggle to dig out the names of those schools and employers—make sure they’re immediately <span class="no-hyphens">visible.</span>

In this revision, the visual emphasis has shifted from the headings—who cares about résumé headings?—to the substance. This also makes the résumé more skimmable, which is always a good <span class="no-hyphens">thing.</span>

[Production tips for printed résumés](#production-tips-for-printed-rsums)

</div>

I treat a résumé as a special kind of <a href="letterhead.html" class="xref">letterhead</a>. Review those production tips.

Past that, resist the urge to buy paper specially marketed for résumés—the kinds that come in odd colors (e.g., green, pink, gray) or textures (e.g., parchment, marble, linen). High-quality business-letterhead paper from the stationery store works best. Anything more elaborate looks <span class="no-hyphens">overbaked.</span>

For that reason, make sure you know <a href="how-to-make-a-pdf.html" class="xref">how to make a PDF</a>.

Increasingly, employers and recruiters are asking for résumés in PDF format. In PDF, good typography survives; good paper is <span class="no-hyphens">irrelevant.</span>

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- *Résumé* is the original spelling and still preferred. *Resumé* is acceptable. *Resume* is common but wrong. How do you type the é character? See <a href="common-accented-characters.html" class="xref">common accented characters</a>.

- Nothing beats having another person—or even better, more than one—edit your résumé. But insist that each reviewer propose at least three edits, so they don’t return with the chipper-but-useless “Looks great to <span class="no-hyphens">me!”</span>

- If you’re applying for a design job, it’s entirely fair that you be judged in part by the typography of your résumé. While this seems self-evident, I still see an astonishing number of design résumés set in <a href="system-fonts.html" class="xref">system fonts</a>. Confidential to all of you: enough <span class="no-hyphens">already.</span>
