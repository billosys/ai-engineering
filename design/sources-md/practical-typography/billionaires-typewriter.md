The billionaire’s typewriter

A friend pointed me to a story on Medium called [“Death to Typewriters,”](https://medium.com/designing-medium/death-to-typewriters-9b7712847639) by Medium designer [Marcin Wichary.](https://medium.com/@mwichary) The story is about the influence of the typewriter on digital typesetting. It references my “excellent list” of <a href="typewriter-habits.html" class="xref">typewriter habits</a>.

Thank you for the compliment, Mr. Wichary. I can’t quibble with the details of your piece. It’s true that Medium and I are opposed to certain typographic shortcuts imported from the <span class="no-hyphens">typewriter.</span>

But by the end, I realized I disagree deeply with Medium about the ethics of design. And by ethics, I mean something simple: though Medium and I are both making tools for writers, what I want for writers and what Medium wants couldn’t be more different. Medium may be avoiding what made the typewriter bad, but it’s also avoiding what made it good. Writers who are tempted to use Medium—or similar publishing tools—should be conscious of these <span class="no-hyphens">tradeoffs.</span>

So, a few words about <span class="no-hyphens">that.</span>

<div class="gap" style="height:1em">

</div>

For those who don’t incessantly follow internet startups, [Medium](http://medium.com) is a blogging service run by one of the founders of Twitter, multibillionaire [Evan Williams.](https://medium.com/@ev) Though it owes much to blogging services of the past (including [Blogger,](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blogger_%28service%29) also founded by Mr. Williams), Medium is oriented toward longer, less diaristic <span class="no-hyphens">stories.</span>

Medium also differs from earlier blogging services in a significant, contrarian way: it offers you, the writer, nearly zero options for the presentation of your stories. No matter what kind of story you write, or who your readers are, it gets packaged into a single, non-negotiable <span class="no-hyphens">template.</span>

Medium isn’t the only blogging service riding this wave, though so far it seems to have the biggest surfboard. Others include [Svbtle,](http://svbtle.com) [Postagon,](http://postagon.com) and [Silvrback.](http://silvrback.com) They all promote a similarly constrained approach to design, which is sometimes called [minimalist.](https://www.postagon.com)

<div id="minimalist-vs-homogeneous-design" class="subhead">

[Minimalist vs. homogeneous design](#minimalist-vs-homogeneous-design)

</div>

As a fan of minimalism, however, I think that term is misapplied here. Minimalism doesn’t foreclose either expressive breadth or conceptual depth. On the contrary, the minimalist program—as it initially emerged in fine art of the 20th century—has been about diverting the viewer’s attention from overt signs of authorship to the deeper purity of the <span class="no-hyphens">ingredients.</span>

Ad Reinhardt with some of his amazing [black paintings.](http://www.guggenheim.org/new-york/collections/collection-online/artwork/3698) (Spoiler alert: they’re not entirely <span class="no-hyphens">monochromatic.)</span>

Bryan Garner notes that *homogeneous* is frequently misspelled *homogenous*, and has five syllables that are frequently mispronounced as four. ([GMEU 4th ed.](https://www.amazon.com/Garners-Modern-English-Usage-Garner/dp/0190491485/) at <span class="no-hyphens">468.)</span>

If that’s the case, we can’t say that Medium et al. are offering minimalist design. Only the veneer is minimalist. What they’re really offering is a shift from design as a choice to design as a constant. Instead of minimalist design, a better term might be *homogeneous* <span class="no-hyphens">design.</span>

On the one hand, Medium’s homogeneous design works and reads well. Members of Medium’s design team have [catalogued the many typographic details](https://medium.com/designing-medium) they’ve implemented. Good for them. If they [sometimes act](https://medium.com/@verbagetruck/dustin-senos-typesets-the-future-86fd91e9b6ee) as if they discovered typography like it was the Higgs boson, we can forgive their overenthusiasm. Bringing these details to a wider audience, and raising standards for typography on the web generally, is a worthy <span class="no-hyphens">project.</span>

On the other hand, a necessary side effect of Medium’s homogeneous design is that every story looks the same. If you agree that the role of typography is to enhance the text for the benefit of the reader (as I contend in <a href="who-is-typography-for.html" class="xref">who is typography for?</a>), then it stands to reason that different texts demand distinct typography. As I say in <a href="what-is-good-typography.html" class="xref">What is Good Typography?</a>, one size never fits all. Typography wants to be <span class="no-hyphens">heterogeneous.</span>

<div id="delicious-but-not-nutritious" class="subhead">

[Delicious but not nutritious](#delicious-but-not-nutritious)

</div>

Still, I wouldn’t say that Medium’s homogeneous design is bad *ex ante*. Among web-publishing tools, I see Medium as the equivalent of a frozen pizza: not as wholesome as a meal you could make yourself, but for those without the time or motivation to cook, a potentially better option than just eating peanut butter straight from the <span class="no-hyphens">jar.</span>

“You don’t need to see our formatting <span class="no-hyphens">options.”</span>

The problem, however, is that Medium holds out its homogeneous design as more than a frozen pizza. It has become, by the Jedi mind trickery favored by today’s tech companies, a Bellagio buffet of delicious <span class="no-hyphens">nonsense:</span>

1.  Evan Williams [frames Medium](https://medium.com/about/what-were-trying-to-do-with-medium-e2f5bfcf0434) as a “place for ideas” with an “ethos” of “openness and democracy—like the internet itself”. Fine, but idealistic platitudes explain nothing. How, specifically, does Medium improve the <span class="no-hyphens">internet?</span>

2.  Mr. Williams [claims](https://medium.com/about/writing-in-medium-df8eac9f4a5e) that Medium is “the best writing tool on the web”. Okay, that’s at least concrete. But we’ve got a lot of good web-based writing tools already. Medium does more than <span class="no-hyphens">those?</span>

3.  Actually, no—Mr. Williams [concedes](https://medium.com/about/writing-in-medium-df8eac9f4a5e) that Medium has “stripped out a lot of the power that other editors give you”. So how is it possible to be “the best” while offering <span class="no-hyphens">less?</span>

4.  Here, Mr. Williams [parries](https://medium.com/about/writing-in-medium-df8eac9f4a5e)—he claims that thinking about the presentation of your work is “a terrible distraction and a waste of time”. <span class="no-hyphens">Why?</span>

5.  Apparently [because he’s](https://medium.com/about/writing-in-medium-df8eac9f4a5e) “one of those people who will open up Word and spend half \[his\] time defining styles and adjusting the spacing between paragraphs”. Hmm, not everyone has that problem with <span class="no-hyphens">Word.</span>

6.  Now comes the hand-waving, as Mr. Williams assures us that Medium’s homogeneous design isn’t a limitation—it’s [in fact essential](https://medium.com/about/writing-in-medium-df8eac9f4a5e) to let your “brilliance and creativity flow smoothly onto the <span class="no-hyphens">screen”.</span>

7.  Moreover, anyone who disagrees [is a Luddite](https://medium.com/about/writing-in-medium-df8eac9f4a5e)—because “everything \[other than Medium\] feels like stepping back in <span class="no-hyphens">time”.</span>

Like all nonsense, it’s intended to be easy to swallow. But Mr. Williams’s argument is flawed in at least three <span class="no-hyphens">ways:</span>

1.  **It makes no sense in the context of today’s web**. If Medium had launched in 2005, it would’ve been astonishing. But it didn’t. Today, the costs of web publishing—including design—have declined to almost zero. Relative to today’s web, Medium is not creating new possibilities, but instead closing them off. To prevail, Medium needs to persuade you that you don’t care about the broader expressive possibilities of web <span class="no-hyphens">publishing.</span>

2.  **It sets up a false dichotomy about writing tools**. Mr. Williams depicts the writer’s choice as Medium vs. complicated tools like Word. Not accurate. First, different tools exist for different needs. It would be silly to use Word to make a web page, but equally silly to use Medium to prepare a print-on-demand paperback. Second, anyone who’s used current blogging tools appreciates that web publishing has become heavily automated. Much of the formatting can be handled automatically (e.g., via [WordPress themes](https://wordpress.org/themes/)) or manually, as you <span class="no-hyphens">prefer.</span>

3.  **You’re giving up far more than design choice**. Mr. Williams [describes](https://medium.com/about/writing-in-medium-df8eac9f4a5e) Medium’s key benefit as rescuing writers from the “terrible distraction” of formatting chores. But consider the cost. Though he’s baiting the hook with design, he’s also asking you, the writer, to let him control how you offer your work to readers. Meaning, to get the full benefit of Medium’s design, you have to let your story live on Medium, send all your readers to Medium, have your work permanently entangled with other stories on Medium, and so on—a significant <span class="no-hyphens">concession.</span>

As for that entanglement among stories, Mr. Williams [has conceded](https://medium.com/@ev/sarah-lacys-latest-medium-me-critique-makes-no-sense-1917c67405ca) that it’s “confusing”. But this ambiguity isn’t a bug. It’s an essential feature of the business plan. The goal is to create the illusion that everything on Medium belongs to one editorial ecosystem, as if it were the *New York Times*.

No word yet on how Medium’s [surveillance policies](https://medium.com/policy/medium-privacy-policy-f03bf92035c9) square with all that openness and <span class="no-hyphens">democracy.</span>

But unlike the *Times*, Medium pays for [only a small fraction](https://medium.com/@ev/what-is-now-the-matter-at-medium-105a334f2ea) of its stories. The rest are submitted—for free—by writers like you. After a long time [being elusive](http://www.theatlantic.com/technology/archive/2013/08/what-is-medium/278965/) about its business model, Medium revealed that it plans to make money by—surprise!—[selling advertising.](http://adage.com/article/digital/bmw-runs-ads-medium-twitter-founders-platform/294321/) This means displaying ads, but also collecting and selling data about readers and writers. So Medium will extract revenue from every story, whether it paid for that story or not. (By the way, will that revenue be shared with writers? [Um, no.](https://medium.com/inside/there-will-be-money-d80f7d0178a9))

And coming full circle—what’s the indispensable tool for creating this illusion of an editorial ecosystem? The homogeneous design. The butterfly ballot of 2000 (depicted in <a href="why-typography-matters.html" class="xref">Why typography matters</a>) proved that errors of typography can have historic consequences. Medium proves that typography can be used as a tool of economic leverage and <span class="no-hyphens">control.</span>

In truth, Medium’s main product is not a publishing platform, but the promotion of a publishing platform. This promotion brings readers and writers onto the site. This, in turn, generates the usage data that’s valuable to advertisers. Boiled down, Medium is simply marketing in the service of more marketing. It is not a “place for ideas”. It is a place for advertisers. It is, therefore, utterly <span class="no-hyphens">superfluous.</span>

“But what about all the writing on Medium?” The measure of superfluity is not the writing on Medium. Rather, it’s what Medium *adds* to the writing. Recall the question from above: how does Medium improve the internet? I haven’t seen a single story on Medium that couldn’t exist equally well elsewhere. Nor evidence that Medium’s editing and publishing tools are a manifest improvement over what you can do with other <span class="no-hyphens">tools.</span>

In sum—still <span class="no-hyphens">superfluous.</span>

<div id="what-we-can-learn-from-typewriters" class="subhead">

[What we can learn from typewriters](#what-we-can-learn-from-typewriters)

</div>

Olivetti was famous for its [graphic and advertising design](https://www.flickr.com/photos/19855494@N00/sets/72157603713300507/) as well as its <span class="no-hyphens">typewriters.</span>

IBM invented the “type ball” technology that made it possible to use different fonts in a typewriter. I studied this one as part of my research for .

Let’s remember two points that get lost among the torches and pitchforks carried by [“Death to <span class="no-hyphens">Typewriters.”</span>](https://medium.com/designing-medium/death-to-typewriters-9b7712847639)

First, although the typewriter did impose homogeneous (and ugly) typography, it had excellent ethics. The typewriter made it possible to write more quickly, legibly, and accurately than ever before, with low cost and high portability. In short, it offered freedom. For that, homogeneous design was a small price to <span class="no-hyphens">pay.</span>

Second, though typewriter typography was terrible, it wasn’t a choice made by typewriter manufacturers out of laziness or ignorance. These compromises were necessitated by the mechanical limitations of the typewriter. Typewriters were never ideal, but as certain limitations were overcome, they got <span class="no-hyphens">better.</span>

With today’s networked computers, we’re getting closer to the ideal. We enjoy the benefits of the typewriter without any of its limitations. We get more efficiency, speed, storage, design options, and freedom. The computer is the most remarkable device in the 500-year history of printing (which already includes a lot of remarkable <span class="no-hyphens">devices).</span>

This leads back to why those <a href="typewriter-habits.html" class="xref">typewriter habits</a> are so awful in the digital age. Computers have none of the mechanical limitations of typewriters. So the typographic shortcuts that were a necessary evil with typewriters are likewise obsolete. Why perpetuate <span class="no-hyphens">them?</span>

For more on this, see [“Reversing the Tide of Declining <span class="no-hyphens">Expectations.”</span>](http://typo.la/rtde)

I rely on a broader version of this principle in my own work. Technology keeps improving, thereby expanding possibilities for us. So we have a choice. We can either ignore those possibilities, and merely accept what technology offers, which will ultimately make us lazy. Or we can explore those new possibilities. But to do that, we need to expect more of <span class="no-hyphens">ourselves.</span>

We also need better tools. I’d characterize most of my work as toolsmithing—whether the project is [designing a font,](https://mbtype.com/fonts/concourse/) [writing a book,](http://practicaltypography.com) or [creating publishing software.](http://pollenpub.com) I don’t control how others use these tools. I don’t want to, either. For me, it’s far more interesting to share these tools and then be surprised by how others use <span class="no-hyphens">them.</span>

To that end, I deliberately avoid creating tools that do too much. Some assembly is always required. For instance, I’ll tell you the qualities of good <a href="websites.html" class="xref">website</a> typography, but I’m not going to sell you a template. I want the customers for my tools to be responsible for some of the heavy lifting. That way, they discover that what they get out of the tool has a connection to what they put <span class="no-hyphens">in.</span>

So even though I oppose the <a href="typewriter-habits.html" class="xref">typewriter habits</a>, I still appreciate that core ethic of the typewriter—removing limitations when you can, doing your best with them when you can’t. That’s a great idea. Yes, let’s explore all the possibilities of the technology available to us. Let’s hack the hell out of everything and see what happens. In the typewriter era, the technological limitations were mostly hardware. Today, mostly software. But if we treat these limitations as something to obey—not overcome—we’ll just become indentured to whoever controls that <span class="no-hyphens">technology.</span>

<div id="what-we-can-learn-from-medium" class="subhead">

[What we can learn from Medium](#what-we-can-learn-from-medium)

</div>

In [“Death to Typewriters,”](https://medium.com/designing-medium/death-to-typewriters-9b7712847639) Medium insists that the typewriter is its “sworn enemy”. In certain typographic details, maybe so. But as a device that imposes homogeneous design, Medium still has a lot in common with the <span class="no-hyphens">typewriter.</span>

In fact, its ethics are actually *worse* than the traditional typewriter. Why? Because Medium’s homogeneous design has nothing to do with limitations of the underlying technology (in this case, the web). As discussed above, it’s a deliberate choice that lets Medium extract value from the talent and labor of <span class="no-hyphens">others.</span>

Medium is a new kind of typewriter—the billionaire’s typewriter. It’s not the only billionaire’s typewriter. So is the Kindle. So is iBooks. So is Twitter. What distinguishes these new typewriters is not the possibilities they make available to writers, but what they take <span class="no-hyphens">away.</span>

Whereas the traditional typewriter offered freedom at the cost of design, the billionaire’s typewriter offers convenience at the cost of <span class="no-hyphens">freedom.</span>

As a writer and toolsmith, I’ve found the rush to embrace these systems perplexing. Not because I’m curmudgeonly. Not because I fail to understand that people, including writers, enjoy things that are free and <span class="no-hyphens">convenient.</span>

Rather, because gentle scrutiny reveals that these systems are powered by a form of human fracking. To get his fracking permit on your territory, Mr. Williams (the multibillionaire) needs to persuade you (the writer) that a key consideration in your work (namely, how & where you offer it to readers) is a “waste of <span class="no-hyphens">time”.</span>

If you really believe that, then by all means, keep using the billionaire’s <span class="no-hyphens">typewriter.</span>

<div class="gap" style="height:1em">

</div>

But if you have doubts, here’s a <span class="no-hyphens">counterproposal.</span>

As a writer, the biggest potential waste of your time is not typography chores, but Medium itself. Because in return for that snazzy design, Medium needs you to relinquish control of how your work gets to <span class="no-hyphens">readers.</span>

Tempting perhaps. But where does it lead? I fear that writers who limit themselves to providing “content” for someone else’s “branded platform” are going to end up with as much leverage as cows on a dairy farm. (A problem at the core of the recent [Hachette–Amazon dispute.](http://www.vanityfair.com/news/business/2014/12/amazon-hachette-ebook-publishing))

If you want to be part of something open and democratic, use open-source software. If you want to have your writing look great, learn something about typography (or hire a designer). If you need a platform for writing, try [Pollen](http://pollenpub.com) (the system I made for this site), or [WordPress,](http://wordpress.org) or a subscription service like [Atavist.](http://atavist.com) I prefer web publishing despite [its shortcomings,](economics-year-one.md) but if you don’t, then make an e-book or PDF and distribute it <span class="no-hyphens">yourself.</span>

As writers, we don’t need companies like Medium to tell us how to use the web. Or define openness and democracy. Or tell us what’s a “waste of \[our\] time” and what’s not. Or determine how and where readers experience our work. We need to decide those things for <span class="no-hyphens">ourselves.</span>

—Matthew Butterick  
17 Feb <span class="no-hyphens">2015</span>

<div id="update" class="subhead">

[Update, 14 months later](#update-14-months-later)

</div>

Perhaps my comparison of Medium to “frozen pizza”—a fan favorite—eventually reached the desk of Evan Williams. Because in a recent interview with the Guardian, he derides today’s web as largely [“junk food”.](http://www.theguardian.com/media/2016/apr/10/twitter-ev-williams-medium-content-fast-food) This is ironic, of course, because as the founder of Twitter, Mr. Williams is basically the [Ray Kroc](http://www.mcdonalds.com/us/en/our_story/our_history/the_ray_kroc_story.html) of the <span class="no-hyphens">web.</span>

But never mind that. Medium, [he explains,](http://www.theguardian.com/media/2016/apr/10/twitter-ev-williams-medium-content-fast-food) is about serving “healthy, nourishing food”. Even the front page of the site says so: Medium isn’t just publishing web pages—it’s going to [“move thinking forward”](https://medium.com) in ways that, apparently, the open web <span class="no-hyphens">cannot.</span>

Maybe it’s no surprise that Mr. Williams is reaching for the ethical high ground—Medium seems to be struggling. The past year has seen several <span class="no-hyphens">reversals:</span>

Medium also swapped out its <a href="body-text.html" class="xref">body text</a> font: instead of the distinctive (but expensive) [Freight Text,](https://www.dardenstudio.com/typefaces/freight_text) it now uses <a href="charter.html" class="xref">Charter</a>, which I’ve been distributing for free since <span class="no-hyphens">2013.</span>

1.  After launching Medium [without a comment system](https://medium.com/@ev/why-medium-notes-are-different-and-how-to-use-them-well-5972c72b18f2#.8ev57c6z4)—because that was the junk food of 1999—last year Mr. Williams [relented and added comments,](https://medium.com/@ev/responses-are-not-free-for-all-comments-6c9a4927c03#.kuotcsy2y) making Medium more like every other blog (or more like Twitter without a character <span class="no-hyphens">limit).</span>

2.  After positioning Medium as a [publisher of original writing](https://gigaom.com/2013/05/07/evan-williams-on-medium-the-magazine-is-the-analog-for-what-were-doing/) by [acquiring web publisher Matter,](https://medium.com/@readmatter/matter-and-medium-c41584ab5fcf#.d2rgo8gwa) Mr. Williams recently spun it out of Medium to become [his hobby investment.](http://www.capitalnewyork.com/article/media/2016/03/8594285/medium-spins-matter-new-company-owned-ev-williams) Other Medium properties that had featured new writing, like [Re:form](https://medium.com/re-form) and [Archipelago,](https://medium.com/the-archipelago) have been shut down <span class="no-hyphens">altogether.</span>

3.  After trying to hatch a [highfalutin advertising business](http://adage.com/article/digital/bmw-runs-ads-medium-twitter-founders-platform/294321/) premised on Medium driving traffic to the publishers under its umbrella, Medium is now repositioning itself as a highfalutin [WordPress alternative.](https://medium.com/the-story/making-medium-more-powerful-for-publishers-39663413a904#.iljevrpjm) The new business model? Rather than Medium sending traffic to publishers, publishers will share their traffic—and revenue—[with <span class="no-hyphens">Medium.</span>](https://medium.com/the-story/revenue-on-medium-5e7e6218f70c#.lk9zqaez9)

“Wait—*what* revenue?” Um, yeah. When a venture-funded tech startup is looking to hitch its wagon to a team of starving mares, it’s [not a good sign.](http://observer.com/2016/04/medium-for-publishers-will-be-a-hard-sell/) Especially because the myth that has been attached to Medium—primarily [by Mr. Williams](http://recode.net/2015/12/24/mediums-ev-williams-its-going-to-make-less-and-less-sense-to-publish-on-the-web/)—is that Medium is the “future of publishing” or similar <span class="no-hyphens">nonsense.</span>

Why nonsense? Because the evidence has been rolling in, and it doesn’t support Medium’s grandiose theory of its own indispensability. Instead, the open web is still winning. That’s great <span class="no-hyphens">news.</span>

The continuing absence of a revenue model, however, is not great news. But that’s going to be [on us to solve,](why-theres-no-e-book-or-pdf.md) not <span class="no-hyphens">Medium.</span>

With its various pivots, Medium has essentially conceded that it doesn’t have a better idea how to make money from web publishing. Its “new” idea is as ancient as human commerce: position yourself in the middle, where you can put your hands in other people’s pockets. At Medium, the future of publishing looks increasingly like its <span class="no-hyphens">past.</span>

<div class="gap" style="height:2em">

</div>

When I originally wrote this piece, I didn’t understand the motivations of Mr. Williams. It seemed to me that if you were a billionaire who really wanted to “move thinking forward”, you’d have so many wonderful options—fund scholarships, endow professorships, start research programs, open nonprofit organizations. Yet Mr. Williams chose to sidestep all those possibilities. Instead, he created another tech startup. The billionaire’s <span class="no-hyphens">typewriter.</span>

But after ten months of the Donald Trump presidential campaign, these motivations are perhaps less mysterious. As a candidate, Mr. Trump has [no inherent interest](http://nymag.com/daily/intelligencer/2016/04/inside-the-donald-trump-presidential-campaign.html) in the details of public policy and coalition-building that are central to political action. Rather, he’s insecure about his public reputation—[inept investor,](https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/wonk/wp/2015/09/03/if-donald-trump-followed-this-really-basic-advice-hed-be-a-lot-richer/) reality-TV buffoon, [short-fingered vulgarian](http://www.vanityfair.com/culture/2015/10/graydon-carter-donald-trump)—and sees his presidential campaign as his last, best [chance to be taken seriously.](https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/post-politics/wp/2016/04/02/transcript-donald-trump-interview-with-bob-woodward-and-robert-costa/) How? By barging into the conversation about our nation’s most consequential <span class="no-hyphens">issues.</span>

I won’t speculate about Mr. Williams in particular—who [dropped out of college](http://www.theguardian.com/media/2016/apr/10/twitter-ev-williams-medium-content-fast-food) because he considered it a [“waste of time”,](http://www.nytimes.com/2009/03/08/jobs/08bosses.html) whose first successful company [nearly died,](http://web.archive.org/web/20020205070520/http://www.evhead.com/longer/2200706_essays.asp) and whose second successful company was the accidental offshoot of his [failed startup.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odeo) But any wealthy person who wants to orchestrate a conversation about the future of publishing—from a perch of significant self-interest—deserves commensurate scrutiny and <span class="no-hyphens">skepticism.</span>

As someone who had a good run in the tech world, I buy the theory that the main reason successful tech founders start another company is to find out if they were smart or merely lucky the first time. Of course, the smart already *know* they were also lucky, so further evidence is unnecessary. It’s only the lucky who want proof they were <span class="no-hyphens">smart.</span>

—Matthew Butterick  
12 April <span class="no-hyphens">2016</span>

<div id="update-another-three-years-on" class="subhead">

[Update, another three years on](#update-another-three-years-on)

</div>

I haven’t followed Medium closely. But Laura Owen has, and this week published a [comprehensive seven-year summary](http://www.niemanlab.org/2019/03/the-long-complicated-and-extremely-frustrating-history-of-medium-2012-present/) of Medium’s many pivots and reversals. Most recently, they’ve been pushing \$50/year reader subscriptions. I took a look to see what I’d <span class="no-hyphens">missed.</span>

What stands out the most? Though Medium’s value to online publishing has remained abstract, the harm it has inflicted—especially upon the authors and publications who have been lured onto the platform (by one pivot) and then stranded (by the next)—is concrete. As writer Jaya Saxena [put it,](https://twitter.com/jayasax/status/994252016129101824) “every writer/cartoonist I know has had their life ruined by Medium at some <span class="no-hyphens">point”.</span>

Yet Mr. Williams [continues to insist](https://www.nytimes.com/2018/05/09/business/ev-williams-twitter-medium.html)—against what is now years of contrary evidence—that Medium will “fix the internet”. He even sent a [“mini-manifesto”](https://static01.nyt.com/files/2018/business/Ev_Williams_Document.pdf) to the *New York Times* asserting that there is a sustainable model for internet writing that is completely “ad-free”—also against years of contrary evidence. Lately, Mr. Williams has been seeking a new supply [of other people’s money](https://www.businessinsider.com/ev-williams-raising-more-money-for-medium-2018-11) to keep Medium afloat, apparently because [\$132 million](https://www.crunchbase.com/search/funding_rounds/field/organizations/funding_total/medium) wasn’t <span class="no-hyphens">enough.</span>

Despite my critique of Medium’s ethics, I respect Mr. Williams’s willingness to experiment. He doesn’t owe writers anything. It’s not like he bought *New York* magazine and ruined it. (Though, [hmm.](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-12-13/medium-s-ev-williams-is-said-to-discuss-buying-new-york-magazine)) Any writer [who partnered with Medium](https://digiday.com/media/steven-levy-medium-tech/) expecting that a venture-funded tech startup would offer the stability of, say, Condé Nast deserved [whatever they got.](https://www.recode.net/2016/4/29/11586552/steven-levy-medium-backchannel-conde-nast) Like [Axl Rose](https://www.gq.com/story/final-comeback-axl-rose) during the recording of *Chinese Democracy*, Mr. Williams has used his celebrity status to pursue ideas that would never be tolerated from other tech founders. There can be virtue in that <span class="no-hyphens">effort.</span>

And yet. Throughout the pivots chronicled by Ms. Owen, one crucial aspect of Medium’s concept has remained largely unchanged: the idea of heterogeneous writing packaged into homogeneous design. Even when Medium launched, this wasn’t original. But during Medium’s early years, the design and typography were sufficiently elevated to make the site distinctive. Since then, a zillion self-described [“Medium clones”](https://github.com/gothinkster/realworld) have been unleashed. Meanwhile, Medium allowed its design to stagnate, thereby forfeiting its most visible competitive <span class="no-hyphens">advantage.</span>

This is especially curious because in all other ways, Medium has grown to resemble the clickbaity ad-driven websites that Mr. Williams claims to disdain. Has he really not noticed? The mountains of atrocious writing. The relentless [wheedling popups.](effluents-influence-affluence.md) The inane comment threads. Under Mr. Williams’s leadership, Medium has essentially become a subscription version of HuffPost. (Well, that’s not totally fair—HuffPost removed its comment threads a while <span class="no-hyphens">back.)</span>

[Mr. Williams proclaims](https://static01.nyt.com/files/2018/business/Ev_Williams_Document.pdf) that the “quality of the information” matters more than “quantity of attention”. But you’d never guess that from actually visiting Medium. For instance, someone clicking on the heading [**Culture**](https://medium.com/topic/culture) today hoping for something befitting the name—ballet? opera? book reviews?—would instead find among the top ten <span class="no-hyphens">links:</span>

- [*Can High Times Survive in the Age of <span class="no-hyphens">Vaping?</span>*](https://onezero.medium.com/can-the-bible-of-weed-survive-in-the-age-of-vaping-6a1baf1b7e4e?source=topic_page---------------------------20)

- [*Tools for More Effective Influencer <span class="no-hyphens">Marketing</span>*](https://medium.com/@wearemarketing/make-sure-your-consumers-know-its-an-ad-tools-for-more-effective-influencer-marketing-cff1fe332970?source=topic_page---------6------------------1)

- [*I Was an Intern on a Celebrity Gossip Show in New <span class="no-hyphens">York</span>*](https://theascent.pub/i-was-an-intern-on-a-celebrity-gossip-show-in-new-york-58e68a0c0af4?source=topic_page---------8------------------1)

It’s apparent that a large percentage of Medium stories (like \#2 and \#3 above) are placed by authors who see them as ads for their off-Medium businesses. Against that backdrop, [Mr. Williams’s claim](https://static01.nyt.com/files/2018/business/Ev_Williams_Document.pdf) that Medium is an “ad-free platform” is either comically obtuse or willfully blind. Medium is full of ads! The twist is that instead of charging advertisers for these ads, he’s charging <span class="no-hyphens">readers.</span>

Which brings us to the [\$132 million](https://www.crunchbase.com/search/funding_rounds/field/organizations/funding_total/medium) question: who’s going to pay [\$50 every year](https://medium.com/membership) for this? People dissatisfied with the unlimited free clickbait available elsewhere? Gulls, rubes, and saps? Dogs with credit cards? Mr. Williams has always preferred to frame Medium in gauzy platitudes—what I called his “Bellagio buffet of delicious nonsense”. Once upon a time, this seemed like the benign puffery of the ultrawealthy. Billionaire-splaining. Today, as the gulf between the promise of Medium and the reality yawns ever larger, it seems like abject denial. Again—has Mr. Williams *actually spent time* on Medium recently? If so, it hasn’t made a dent. If not, I can hardly blame him. It’s <span class="no-hyphens">awful.</span>

—Matthew Butterick  
29 March <span class="no-hyphens">2019</span>
