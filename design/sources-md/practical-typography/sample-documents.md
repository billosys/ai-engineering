Sample documents

Fonderie S. Berthier type specimen, Paris, <span class="no-hyphens">1883</span>

Now it’s time to put everything together. Like good writing, good typography isn’t determined by elements in isolation but by the relationship of those elements and the effect of the <span class="no-hyphens">whole.</span>

So far we’ve looked at typographic rules individually. In this chapter, we’ll look at these rules in the context of some common documents. Your goal is to get better at seeing typographic flaws in layouts and fixing <span class="no-hyphens">them.</span>

The names and situations in the sample documents are, of course, <span class="no-hyphens">fictional.</span>

Each sample document is shown as a before-and-after demonstration: “before” represents a typical layout with the usual bad habits; “after” represents a layout upgraded with the lessons of this <span class="no-hyphens">book.</span>

Except for <a href="websites.html" class="xref">websites</a>, the samples in this chapter were created with Microsoft Office on Mac OS. Not my favorite design tool, but its typographic capabilities are typical of those available to the professional writer. If I can make good typography under those conditions, you can <span class="no-hyphens">too.</span>
