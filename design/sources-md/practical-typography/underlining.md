underliningAbsolutely not

In a printed document, don’t underline. Ever. It’s ugly and it makes text harder to read. See for <span class="no-hyphens">yourself—</span>

<div style="margin-left:2.5rem">

<span style="border-bottom:0.15em solid black">Underlining is another dreary <a href="typewriter-habits.html" class="xref">typewriter habit</a>. Typewriters had no bold or italic styling. So the only way to emphasize text was to back up the carriage and type underscores beneath the text. It was a workaround for shortcomings in typewriter <span class="no-hyphens">technology.</span></span>

Underlining is another dreary <a href="typewriter-habits.html" class="xref">typewriter habit</a>. Typewriters had no bold or italic styling. So the only way to emphasize text was to back up the carriage and type underscores beneath the text. It was a workaround for shortcomings in typewriter <span class="no-hyphens">technology.</span>

</div>

Neither your word processor nor your web browser suffers from these shortcomings. If you feel the urge to underline, use <a href="bold-or-italic.html" class="xref">bold or italic</a> instead. In special situations, like <a href="headings.html" class="xref">headings</a>, you can also consider <a href="all-caps.html" class="xref">all caps</a>, <a href="small-caps.html" class="xref">small caps</a>, or changing the <a href="point-size.html" class="xref">point size</a>.

Not convinced? I invite you to find a book, newspaper, or magazine that underlines text. Aside from supermarket tabloids—was that the look you were going for?—you won’t find <span class="no-hyphens">any.</span>

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- Another reason underlining looks worse than bold or italic: underlining is mechanically applied by the word processor. Bold and italic styles are specially designed to match the regular style of the <span class="no-hyphens">font.</span>

- The “track changes” feature of your word processor will underline text added to the document. This is fine. In fact, it’s one more reason not to use underlining for emphasis—so readers don’t confuse text that’s marked as a revision with text that happens to be <span class="no-hyphens">underlined.</span>

- On the web, hyperlinks have traditionally been underlined. But [email lost its hyphen,](https://www.washingtonpost.com/opinions/the-post-drops-the-mike--and-the-hyphen-in-e-mail/2015/12/04/ccd6e33a-98fa-11e5-8917-653b65c809eb_story.html) and [the internet lost its capitalization.](https://www.nytimes.com/2016/06/02/insider/now-it-is-official-the-internet-is-over.html) Consistent with those signs of maturity, I think it’s time to move beyond underlined hyperlinks <span class="no-hyphens">too.</span>

  “Oh come on! Everyone underlines links!” Some who use underlining sparingly, and often not at all: the *New York Times*, *New York* magazine, the *Washington Post*, the *Guardian*, the *Wall Street Journal*, the *Atlantic*, the *New Yorker*, the *Hollywood Reporter*, Bloomberg, Google, Politico, Amazon, Apple, Microsoft, GitHub, Yahoo News, and Wikipedia. Even eBay, paragon of ’90s <a href="websites.html" class="xref">website</a> design, has relented. Are underlined links dead? Maybe not quite. Dying? For <span class="no-hyphens">sure.</span>

  A vocal minority continues to insist—against logic and years of evidence—that these are merely fringe exceptions, and that I belong to a cult devoted to ruining the web by camouflaging all those precious links. Please take this tinfoil hat, with my <span class="no-hyphens">compliments.</span>
