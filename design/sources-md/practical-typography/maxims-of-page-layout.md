maxims of page layoutPrinciples of balance and consistency

Successful typography requires you to pay attention to the whole, not just the parts. These maxims summarize the key principles I keep in mind when I’m laying out a <span class="no-hyphens">document.</span>

1.  **Decide first how the body text will look.**  
    Why? Because there’s more <a href="body-text.html" class="xref">body text</a> than anything else. Four decisions—<a href="point-size.html" class="xref">point size</a>, <a href="line-length.html" class="xref">line length</a>, <a href="line-spacing.html" class="xref">line spacing</a>, and font—largely determine the appearance of the body text. Therefore, these decisions have the biggest influence on the legibility of the text and the overall appearance of the <span class="no-hyphens">document.</span>

2.  **Divide the page into foreground and background.**  
    The foreground contains the most important page elements. (Hint: the body text is usually one of them.) The background contains everything else. Don’t let the background elements upstage the foreground elements. And remember that you have a limited number of tools for making distinctions: position, size, font, and sometimes color. (See <a href="letterhead.html" class="xref">letterhead</a> for an example of how to handle the foreground–background <span class="no-hyphens">relationship.)</span>

3.  **Make adjustments with the smallest visible increments.**  
    Typography thrives on fine details. The difference between not enough and too much can be <span class="no-hyphens">small.</span>

4.  **When in doubt, try it both ways.**  
    Don’t try to resolve typographic decisions with logic. There’s no substitute for making samples of two options and getting a visual <span class="no-hyphens">reaction.</span>

5.  **Be consistent.**  
    Typography quietly describes to readers a structure and hierarchy. Things that are the same should look the same. Things that look different should actually be different. Without consistent treatment of similar elements, the document will come across as random and <span class="no-hyphens">meandering.</span>

6.  **Relate each new element to existing elements.**  
    The only time you have unfettered discretion is when the page is blank. After that, the page is like a jigsaw puzzle that becomes more constrained with each new piece. A <a href="grids.html" class="xref">grid</a> can help organize this <span class="no-hyphens">process.</span>

7.  **Keep it simple.**  
    A principle as true in typography as anything else. If you think you need three colors and five fonts, think again. If you think you need a logo in the upper left corner of every page, think again. If you think you need to clutter the edges of the page with useless nonsense, think <span class="no-hyphens">again.</span>

8.  **Imitate what you like.**  
    Why reinvent the wheel? If you see typography you like—in a book, on a sign, at a website—emulate it. Learning to see what’s good about other examples of typography makes it easier to solve problems in your own <span class="no-hyphens">layouts.</span>

9.  **Don’t fear white space.**  
    A lot of mediocre typography results from a perceived need to fill space. Things get too big or spread out. Work outward from the text, not inward from the page edges. If the text looks good, the white space will take care of <span class="no-hyphens">itself.</span>
