# Quick Definition

Subtractive colour mixing is the process of combining pigments or dyes that each absorb (subtract) portions of the light spectrum, where combining more colours absorbs more light and produces darker results.

# Core Definition

Drasner introduces subtractive mixing through the contrast with digital work: "As a child, you're working with paint. Paint and inks from a printer have tiny particles called pigments that mix together and reflect to present color to your eye. This is subtractive color mixing. The more colors you add to it, the darker it becomes, until we get brown. Primaries are close to what you're used to: red, yellow, blue. But when you mix these colors with subtractive color mixing, you arrive at brown." The mechanism is that each pigment absorbs certain wavelengths and reflects others; combining pigments removes more wavelengths from the reflected light, reducing total luminance and shifting toward grey or black.

# Prerequisites

- None \u2014 this is a foundational physical concept

# Key Properties

1. Adding colours decreases brightness \u2014 more pigment = darker, closer to black
2. Traditional primaries are red, yellow, blue (artists); professional print uses cyan, magenta, yellow (CMY)
3. Combining all pigments produces near-black (or in paint, brown)
4. Pigments work by absorbing (subtracting) light; the reflected remainder is what we see as colour

# Construction / Recognition

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Physical media \u2014 paint, ink, markers, dyes \u2014 use subtractive mixing
2. Mixing more colours produces a darker, muddier result
3. Red + blue = purple; red + yellow = orange (not the result you get from light)

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Traditional art media, print production (CMYK), textile dyeing, photographic colour processes
- **Common applications**: Understanding why CMYK is used in print (not RGB); understanding why design tools convert RGB to CMYK for print output

# Examples

**Example 1** (from source): "The more colors you add to it, the darker it becomes, until we get brown" \u2014 Drasner's description of what happens when mixing many paint colours together, contrasted with white light as the additive complement.

# Relationships

## Builds Upon
- **Physics of pigments and light absorption** \u2014 Subtractive mixing reflects how pigment particles selectively absorb wavelengths

## Related
- **CMYK colour model** \u2014 The print industry colour model based on subtractive mixing with cyan, magenta, yellow (and black) inks

## Contrasts With
- **Additive colour mixing** \u2014 Red + green = yellow (light); red + green in paint = brown (pigment); all colours combined in light = white; all in pigment = near-black

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Applying paint-mixing intuition to digital colour selection.
  **Correction**: On screen, mixing red and green produces yellow, not brown. Digital colour is additive.
