business cardsShrink and simplify

Business cards have to fit a lot of information in a small area. But they often try to do too <span class="no-hyphens">much.</span>

For instance, the card layout below is fairly common. I call it the baseball-diamond layout: information is pushed out to the corners in the misguided belief that the shape of the card should dictate the shape of the typography. As I mentioned in <a href="maxims-of-page-layout.html" class="xref">maxims of page layout</a>, this technique tends to produce mediocre <span class="no-hyphens">results.</span>

<a href="samples/business-card-before.pdf" class="sample-document"></a>

<div id="before" class="subhead">

[Before](#before)

</div>

1.  <a href="goofy-fonts.html" class="xref">Goofy fonts</a> used for all <span class="no-hyphens">text.</span>

    <a href="bad-fonts.html" class="xref">bad font</a> (Copperplate) used for other <span class="no-hyphens">text.</span>

    <a href="point-size.html" class="xref">Point size</a> of name too <span class="no-hyphens">large.</span>

2.  No <a href="letterspacing.html" class="xref">letterspacing</a> in <span class="no-hyphens">caps.</span>

3.  Too much information, pushed out to <span class="no-hyphens">corners.</span>

The guiding principles with business cards are the same as with <a href="letterhead.html" class="xref">letterhead</a>. Remove anything nonessential. Don’t worry about the text being small—there’s not very much of it. Build the layout from the text outward. The white space will take care of itself. If you work from the edges of the card inward, you’re more likely to end up with a baseball <span class="no-hyphens">diamond.</span>

<a href="samples/business-card-after.pdf" class="sample-document"></a>

<div id="after" class="subhead">

[After](#after)

</div>

1.  used for all text, rather than Goofy Sans and Ugly <span class="no-hyphens">Script.</span>

2.  Point size more reasonable and <span class="no-hyphens">consistent.</span>

3.  Letterspacing added to small <span class="no-hyphens">caps.</span>

4.  Text layout <span class="no-hyphens">simplified.</span>

5.  Pomposity <span class="no-hyphens">eliminated.</span>

<div id="production-tips-for-business-cards" class="subhead">

[Production tips for business cards](#production-tips-for-business-cards)

</div>

See the notes under <a href="letterhead.html" class="xref">letterhead</a> for general tips about getting stationery items designed and <span class="no-hyphens">printed.</span>

In addition, carefully consider the paper stock for your business cards. More than other printed items, business cards provide a tactile experience, much like shaking someone’s hand. A business card should feel great between your fingers. I’ve gotten too many that felt like valet-parking <span class="no-hyphens">receipts.</span>

Professional printing also lets you use smaller point sizes that won’t cause temporary <span class="no-hyphens">blindness.</span>

For that reason, I can’t endorse laser-printed business cards. Those sheets of perforated cards designed for laser printing are miserably thin, and the resulting business cards are flimsy and sad. Forget it. Get them professionally printed, either locally or through an internet <span class="no-hyphens">printer.</span>

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- <a href="color.html" class="xref">Color</a> can be a nice touch on business cards, but it has to be understated. The louder the color, the less of it you can use, and vice <span class="no-hyphens">versa.</span>

- Creative types, I don’t want to dampen your enthusiasm for subverting paradigms. But I can’t endorse stunt cards either—for instance, those that are printed on sheet metal, or that are oversized, or that list you as “evil supergenius”. (Three of many I’ve received.) Keep it <span class="no-hyphens">simple.</span>
