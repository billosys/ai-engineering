all capsFine for less than one line

All-caps text—meaning text with all the letters capitalized—is best used <span class="no-hyphens">sparingly.</span>

At standard <a href="body-text.html" class="xref">body text</a> sizes, capital letters—or simply caps—are harder to read than normal lowercase text. Why? We read more lowercase text, so as a matter of habit, lowercase is more familiar and thus more legible. Furthermore, cognitive research has suggested that the shapes of lowercase letters—some tall (d h k l), some short (a e n s), some descending (g y p q)—create a varied visual contour that helps our brain recognize words. Capitalization homogenizes these shapes, leaving a rectangular <span class="no-hyphens">contour.</span>

That doesn’t mean you shouldn’t use caps. But use them judiciously. Caps are suitable for headings shorter than one line (e.g., “Table of Contents”), headers, footers, captions, or other labels. Caps work at small <a href="point-size.html" class="xref">point sizes</a>. Caps work well on <a href="letterhead.html" class="xref">letterhead</a> and <a href="business-cards.html" class="xref">business cards</a>. Always add <a href="letterspacing.html" class="xref">letterspacing</a> to caps to make them easier to read, and make sure <a href="kerning.html" class="xref">kerning</a> is turned <span class="no-hyphens">on.</span>

DON’T CAPITALIZE WHOLE PARAGRAPHS. THIS HABIT ORIGINATED WITH LAWYERS AND HAS INFECTED SOCIETY AT LARGE. MANY WRITERS SEEM TO THINK THAT CAPITALIZATION COMMUNICATES AUTHORITY AND IMPORTANCE. “HEY, LOOK HERE, I’VE GOT SOMETHING IMPORTANT TO SAY! I DEMAND THAT YOU PAY ATTENTION TO THIS!” BUT A PARAGRAPH SET IN ALL CAPS IS VERY HARD TO READ. **IT’S EVEN WORSE IN BOLD. AS THE PARAGRAPH WEARS ON, READERS FATIGUE. INTEREST WANES. HOW ABOUT YOU? DO YOU ENJOY READING THIS? I DOUBT IT. BUT I REGULARLY SEE CAPITALIZED PARAGRAPHS THAT ARE MUCH LONGER THAN THIS. DO YOUR READERS A FAVOR. STOP CAPITALIZING WHOLE <span class="no-hyphens">PARAGRAPHS.</span>**

The terms uppercase and lowercase come from traditional print shops. Capital letters, used less frequently, were stored in a case on a shelf above the other letters (then called minuscule <span class="no-hyphens">letters).</span>

All-caps paragraphs are an example of self-defeating typography. If you need readers to pay attention to an important part of your document, the last thing you want is for them to skim over it. But that’s what inevitably happens with all-caps paragraphs because they’re so hard to <span class="no-hyphens">read.</span>

To emphasize a paragraph, you have better options. Use <a href="rules-and-borders.html" class="xref">rules and borders</a>. Add a <a href="headings.html" class="xref">heading</a> that labels it **Important**. Run it in a larger <a href="point-size.html" class="xref">point size</a>. But don’t capitalize <span class="no-hyphens">it.</span>

There are two ways to put caps in a document. The popular method is to engage the caps-lock key at the left edge of the keyboard and type away. That works, but it makes capitalization a permanent feature of your <span class="no-hyphens">text.</span>

The preferred method is to apply all-caps formatting to normally typed text. That way, you can toggle capitalization on and off without retyping the text <span class="no-hyphens">itself.</span>

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- Nicer professional fonts include <a href="alternate-figures.html" class="xref">alternate figures</a> and punctuation that are designed to align correctly with caps. These alternates are available as <a href="opentype-features.html" class="xref">OpenType features</a>.

- Caps are often found in legal contracts, like warranties and EULAs, because many lawyers believe that caps are required by contract laws that call for “conspicuous” text. Oh really? The Uniform Commercial Code defines “conspicuous” as “written, displayed, or presented \[so\] that a reasonable person against which it is to operate ought to have noticed it,” but notes that “\[w\]hether a term is ‘conspicuous’ or not is a decision for the court.” (UCC § 1-201(b)(10).) Furthermore, the US Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals has written that “\[l\]awyers who think their caps lock keys are instant ‘make conspicuous’ buttons are deluded. In determining whether a term is conspicuous, we look at more than formatting.” (In re Bassett, 285 F.3d 882, 886 (9th Cir. 2002).) My compliments to those who [take this suggestion <span class="no-hyphens">seriously.</span>](https://www.mozilla.org/MPL/2.0/#disclaimer-of-warranty)

- “Why reject <a href="underlining.html" class="xref">underlining</a> but not caps? Aren’t they both <a href="typewriter-habits.html" class="xref">typewriter habits</a>?” No. Caps are the original alphabetic characters. They are part of the oldest traditions of our written language. Underlining cannot claim a similar pedigree. Caps in English descend directly from the Latin alphabet. (That’s why basic, unstyled fonts are called roman.) Through the early Middle Ages, scribes in Europe adapted the Latin alphabet into smaller, more casual forms, called minuscules. In the 700s, Charlemagne started a project to create a standardized script across his empire. That script, Carolingian minuscule, spread through Europe and popularized the combination of uppercase and lowercase letters that’s been a feature of printed European languages since <span class="no-hyphens">then.</span>

- To those holdouts who type <a href="emails.html" class="xref">emails</a> in all caps: enough already. <span style="text-transform: uppercase">You don’t have to shout. We can hear you just <span class="no-hyphens">fine.</span></span>
