Dear Pro Designers Who Use Adobe <span class="no-hyphens">Software:</span>

You need to stop using <span class="no-hyphens">Minion.</span>

Not because it’s a <a href="bad-fonts.html" class="xref">bad font</a>. I have no complaint with Minion as a work of type <span class="no-hyphens">design.</span>

You need to stop because Minion is not a font choice. It is the absence of a font choice. For many years, Minion has been bundled with Adobe design software. It became the default font starting in CS5. And that’s the main reason you use it. Not because you like it. Rather, because it’s already <span class="no-hyphens">there.</span>

As a typographic shortcut, this is worse than the average computer user who relies on <a href="helvetica-and-arial-alternatives.html" class="xref">arial</a> or <a href="times-new-roman-alternatives.html" class="xref">times new roman</a> or <a href="calibri-alternatives.html" class="xref">calibri</a>. Because unlike the average computer user, you’re supposed to know about typography and better fonts. You’re not supposed to rely on the <span class="no-hyphens">defaults.</span>

I can’t force you to investigate the wide world of professional fonts. But some gentle shaming—that I can <span class="no-hyphens">do.</span>

Imagine what would happen if your clients, or your employer, decided they could get their design projects done by relying on defaults. You’d be out of a job, right? Your work depends on people who care enough to go beyond the defaults and hire <span class="no-hyphens">you.</span>

That’s also true of type designers. They depend on people like you to go beyond default fonts like Minion. And when you don’t—well, maybe you’re applying an inconsistent standard. You don’t want defaults to be good enough for your clients, yet you want them to be good enough for <span class="no-hyphens">you.</span>

The four text faces above—they’re the tiniest tip of the font iceberg. But they’re not Minion. And you’ve got to start <span class="no-hyphens">somewhere.</span>
