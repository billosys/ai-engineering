colorBlack is best

In typography, color is a term with two <span class="no-hyphens">meanings.</span>

First, typographers will sometimes speak of a font as creating a certain color on the page—even when it’s black. Used this way, the word encapsulates a set of hard-to-quantify characteristics like darkness, contrast, rhythm, and <span class="no-hyphens">texture.</span>

The second meaning is the usual one—color as the opposite of black & white. This was once an irrelevant topic, as most of us had to be satisfied with monochrome laser printers. These days, color printers are ubiquitous and more writing is delivered on screen. So color has become a practical <span class="no-hyphens">consideration.</span>

1.  On a page of text, nothing draws the eye more powerfully than a contrast between light and dark colors. This is why a **bold font** creates more emphasis than an *italic font*. (See also <a href="bold-or-italic.html" class="xref">bold or italic</a>.)

2.  The perceived intensity of colored type depends not just on the color, but also the size and weight of the font. So a thin or small font can carry a more intense color than a heavy or large <span class="no-hyphens">font.</span>

3.  I’m not saying it can never be done well, but when someone puts colored type on a colored background, I usually wish they <span class="no-hyphens">hadn’t.</span>

<div id="in-print" class="subhead">

[in print](#in-print)

</div>

<a href="body-text.html" class="xref">body text</a> in printed documents (e.g., <a href="resumes.html" class="xref">résumés</a>, <a href="research-papers.html" class="xref">research papers</a>, letters) must always be set in black type. No <span class="no-hyphens">exceptions.</span>

At a typical body-text <a href="point-size.html" class="xref">point size</a>, color isn’t effective as a form of emphasis. Small letterforms don’t cover much surface area on the page, so colored text isn’t noticed unless it’s <span class="no-hyphens">loud.</span>

In laser-printed documents, avoid white text on a dark background, known as knockout type. Knockout type uses about 20 times more toner than normal, making it an expensive typographic <span class="no-hyphens">effect.</span>

Professionally printed documents (e.g., <a href="letterhead.html" class="xref">letterhead</a>, <a href="business-cards.html" class="xref">business cards</a>) can include text set in color, but use it judiciously. Multiple shades of one color are usually better than multiple contrasting <span class="no-hyphens">colors.</span>

<div id="on-websites" class="subhead">

[on websites](#on-websites)

</div>

The horse may be long out of the barn on this one, but on the web, the same rule of restraint applies: less color is more effective. When everything is emphasized, nothing is <span class="no-hyphens">emphasized.</span>

Consider making your text dark gray rather than black. Unlike a piece of paper—which reflects ambient light—a computer screen projects its own light and tends to have more severe contrast. Therefore, on screen, dark-gray text can be more comfortable to read than black text. That’s why many digital-book readers let you reduce the screen brightness or change the text <span class="no-hyphens">color.</span>

Color remains the idiomatic way to denote clickability on the web. So feel free to use color (with or without <a href="underlining.html" class="xref">underlining</a>) for hyperlinks. But be careful using it on non-clickable text, as it may confuse <span class="no-hyphens">readers.</span>

<div id="in-pdfs" class="subhead">

[in PDFs](#in-pdfs)

</div>

PDFs are read on both screen and paper, so which set of rules you follow depends on how you expect the PDF to be used. If there’s a reasonable chance the PDF will be printed, don’t bother with dark-gray body text—it’ll look gritty and strange when <span class="no-hyphens">printed.</span>

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- Color in <a href="presentations.html" class="xref">presentations</a> is covered in that <span class="no-hyphens">section.</span>

- The human eye can more easily distinguish light colors than dark. This is why a paint store will have 50 shades of white and only two shades of black. So if you’re using light colors, make gentle adjustments; dark colors need bigger <span class="no-hyphens">adjustments.</span>

- Liturgical rubrics are so named because they were originally printed in red. Red has been the favored second color in typography for hundreds of years. To get the most vibrant-looking red, use an old printer’s trick—make it slightly <span class="no-hyphens">orange.</span>

- Color on a printed page is made by two techniques. With spot color, one ink is used to make the color. With process color, four inks are combined (cyan, magenta, yellow, and <span class="no-hyphens">black).</span>

  Spot color is traditionally preferred for projects that involve one or two colors, like letterhead and business cards. Spot color produces the most pure and saturated colors. It also permits special ink effects (e.g., fluorescents, metallics, <span class="no-hyphens">varnishes).</span>

  Color laser printers also use a process-color technique, but they combine colored toners rather than <span class="no-hyphens">inks.</span>

  Process color is used for printing color photographs and other continuous-tone images (jobs for which spot color is ineffective). Process color used to be an expensive technique, mostly restricted to commercial magazines and catalogs. But internet printing services like [MOO](http://moo.com) and [4by6.com](http://4by6.com) have made process color available to <span class="no-hyphens">anyone.</span>

  So why not print everything in process color? The problem is that process color works by layering multiple colors, and changing the balance of those colors by applying a halftone pattern to each. The halftone pattern isn’t visible in color photographs. But it’s visible in type, because it’s a solid color. Halftone patterns also create a gritty edge on small text, which affects legibility. Therefore, process color isn’t ideal for letterhead or business <span class="no-hyphens">cards.</span>

- Pro designers sometimes malign gradient fills as a signifier of amateur design. Like any design tool, they can be used well—or poorly. In the physical world, most of the color we see is essentially a gradient, because natural light falls unevenly. With type, a background gradient that gently changes brightness can give a natural sense of <span class="no-hyphens">dimensionality.</span>
