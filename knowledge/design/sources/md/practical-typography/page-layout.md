Page layout

Page from the Kelmscott Chaucer, printed by William Morris, <span class="no-hyphens">1896.</span>

<a href="type-composition.html" class="xref">Type composition</a> was about picking the right characters. <a href="text-formatting.html" class="xref">Text formatting</a> was about the visual appearance of those characters. Page layout is about the positioning and relationship of text and other elements on the <span class="no-hyphens">page.</span>

In fine printing, typographers usually get to choose the page size of their documents. But you don’t—most of the documents a writer prints will be on standard printer paper. And on the web, your reader essentially chooses the <span class="no-hyphens">size.</span>

That’s no reason to tolerate mediocrity. English artist and printer William Morris famously rebelled against mechanized, mass-produced typography—in the 1890s. He went on to produce a series of beautiful books intended to remind readers and writers what was possible on the printed page, in contrast to the coarse ritual of industrialized <span class="no-hyphens">printing.</span>

Today, the struggle continues. Word processors and web browsers beckon us with default settings and templates that promise great results with no <span class="no-hyphens">effort.</span>

But you only get out what you put in. Don’t accept the defaults. You can do <span class="no-hyphens">better.</span>
