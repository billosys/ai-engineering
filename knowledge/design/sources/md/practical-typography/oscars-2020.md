Oscars 2020: best-picture typography

I live in Hollywood, California. For locals, the Academy Awards are either a) work, if you’re in the movie industry, or b) a week-long traffic nightmare, if you’re anyone <span class="no-hyphens">else.</span>

“I’d like to thank the Academy … for making it difficult to reach <span class="no-hyphens">Doheny”</span>

For many years, the Academy has fibbed about the viewership of the Oscar telecast, [claiming](https://www.oscars.org/sites/oscars/files/92aa_fact_sheet.pdf) “several hundred million” viewers even though [the evidence](https://www.oscars.org/sites/oscars/files/92aa_fact_sheet.pdf) suggests at best 65 <span class="no-hyphens">million.</span>

Among my friends in the entertainment industry, the guild awards (e.g., [DGA,](https://www.dga.org/Awards/Annual.aspx) [WGA,](https://awards.wga.org/awards/nominees-winners) [SAG](https://www.sagawards.org/)) are generally well regarded, the Golden Globes not even a little, and the Academy Awards—well, it’s sort of like the boorish grandparent who comes to visit once a year but gives out \$100 bills to you and your siblings. You grin & bear <span class="no-hyphens">it.</span>

The Academy has no way of knowing whether its voters actually watch the films that are nominated, or just pick a favorite based on some arbitrary criterion. Consistent with that principle, I feel totally justified picking a best-picture winner based on the typography of the <span class="no-hyphens">posters.</span>

**Note for new visitors**: This article is part of a bigger online book called *Practical Typography* written by me, [type designer](https://mbtype.com/) [Matthew Butterick.](https://mbtype.com/bio.html) If you like nerdy typography material, there’s plenty more—see the <a href="index.html" class="xref">table of contents</a>.

<div id="ford-v-ferrari" class="subhead">

[Ford v. Ferrari](#ford-v-ferrari)

</div>

**Movie**: Entertaining, though conventional & <span class="no-hyphens">formulaic.</span>

**Typography**: The narrow sans serif appears to be [Tungsten,](https://www.typography.com/fonts/tungsten/styles) which aptly evokes the mid-’60s. But the positioning of the actor names relative to the title is a little sloppy and <span class="no-hyphens">constricted.</span>

<div id="the-irishman" class="subhead">

[The Irishman](#the-irishman)

</div>

**Movie**: A long slog. The de-aging effect was not <span class="no-hyphens">persuasive.</span>

**Typography**: The font is [Grouch,](https://www.myfonts.com/fonts/bitstream/itc-grouch/) which reflects the [“tight but not touching”](https://www.paulshawletterdesign.com/2011/11/tutorial-no-6%E2%80%94tight-but-not-touching-kerning/) typesetting style that was trendy in the ’70s. A reasonable choice for a film set partially in that decade. The red color, however, contrasts poorly with the bluish <span class="no-hyphens">background.</span>

<div id="jojo-rabbit" class="subhead">

[Jojo Rabbit](#jojo-rabbit)

</div>

**Movie**: Well made, if we overlook some jarring shifts in <span class="no-hyphens">tone.</span>

**Typography**: I don’t recognize the font (it may be custom lettering) but it seems to be evoking a hand-printed, propaganda-poster feel, which feels apt. The poster spares us another swastika, though not the colors of the Nazi flag. The jagged black background behind the title lettering seems to reference [the SS logo.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schutzstaffel) I like that the typography at the top of the poster is given a similar hand-printed <span class="no-hyphens">look.</span>

<div id="joker" class="subhead">

[Joker](#joker)

</div>

**Movie**: Didn’t see <span class="no-hyphens">it.</span>

**Typography**: Like *Jojo Rabbit*, also emulates the look of old-fashioned letterpress printing. I liked finding out that it was [made with real wood type,](https://www.theverge.com/2019/11/15/20961164/joker-dc-film-logo-chad-danieley-artist-art-club) not digital manipulation. The surrounding sans serif is boring and incongruous. Why not continue with the wood <span class="no-hyphens">type?</span>

<div id="little-women" class="subhead">

[Little Women](#little-women)

</div>

**Movie**: Didn’t see <span class="no-hyphens">it.</span>

**Typography**: I really liked the blackletter nameplate for Greta Gerwig’s previous movie, *Lady Bird*. Here, the title lettering is adapted from the cover of the 1868 first edition of *Little Women*. I prefer the 1868 version, however—the contemporized version treads a little close to the overdone trend of [chopping pieces](https://cuttingedgelogos.tumblr.com/) off letterforms. The sans serif used elsewhere appears to be [Avant Garde,](https://www.myfonts.com/fonts/itc/avant-garde-gothic/) and looks terrible because it has no relation to the main <span class="no-hyphens">type.</span>

(HT to [Stephen Coles](https://twitter.com/typographica/) for pointing me to the first <span class="no-hyphens">edition.)</span>

<div id="marriage-story" class="subhead">

[Marriage Story](#marriage-story)

</div>

**Movie**: Didn’t see <span class="no-hyphens">it.</span>

**Typography**: Speaking of chopping pieces from letters. The font seems to be [Bodoni Sans.](https://www.myfonts.com/fonts/greyscale-type/bodoni-sans/) To me, this looks like a perfectly nice serif font that’s been amputated. But somehow, maybe that’s an apt choice for film about a <span class="no-hyphens">divorce.</span>

<div id="1917" class="subhead">

[1917](#1917)

</div>

**Movie**: Fantastic. <span class="no-hyphens">Flawless.</span>

**Typography**: The font is [Futura,](https://www.myfonts.com/fonts/bitstream/futura/) which was released in 1927 and is thus anachronistic for a film set 10 years prior. But hear me out: the movie [prides itself](https://www.theguardian.com/culture/2019/dec/07/sam-mendes-1917-hobnails-drill-boot-camp-war-epic) on its fastidious period authenticity. There were plenty of sans serifs in 1917. They just didn’t look like that. (For instance, my [Hermes Maia](https://mbtype.com/fonts/hermes-maia/) font evokes a style that would’ve been common then.) Still, I think the main reason Futura is used here is that the poster refers to the earlier Sam Mendes movie *Skyfall*, and *Skyfall* used a Futura-like face (namely [Neutraface](https://houseind.com/hi/neutraface)).

<div id="once-upon-a-time-in-hollywood" class="subhead">

[Once Upon a Time in Hollywood](#once-upon-a-time-in-hollywood)

</div>

**Movie**: Hated it. A rancid reactionary fantasia of pre-baby-boomer American <span class="no-hyphens">masculinity.</span>

**Typography**: If you’re going with the most obvious possible typographic idea, you at least have to do it well. I live near the Hollywood sign. Everything about this version is inaccurate. Why? Because the designer merely downloaded a [close-enough freeware font](https://www.dafont.com/hollywood-hills.font) and didn’t even change its wretched spacing (see sample at left, created by me in two <span class="no-hyphens">seconds).</span>

<div id="parasite" class="subhead">

[Parasite](#parasite)

</div>

**Movie**: Started well, but became diffuse after the midway <span class="no-hyphens">point.</span>

**Typography**: The sans serif around the title is [Gotham,](https://www.typography.com/fonts/gotham/how-to-use) and the title itself seems to be Gotham with some serifs glued on, in a manner reminiscent of experimental 1990s fonts like [Dead History.](https://www.emigre.com/Fonts/Dead-History) Could it be that the serifs are meant to seem like “parasites” attached to the sans serif forms, like lampreys on a shark? If so, this concept tries too <span class="no-hyphens">hard.</span>

<div id="and-the-winner-is" class="subhead">

[And the winner is](#and-the-winner-is)

</div>

*1917*, though if I’m voting purely on typography, *Jojo Rabbit*, because it was a concept appropriate for the film, executed well & <span class="no-hyphens">consistently.</span>

—Matthew Butterick  
7 February <span class="no-hyphens">2020</span>
