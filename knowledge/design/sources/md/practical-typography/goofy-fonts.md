goofy fontsDon’t use them

I once met a lawyer who had set his letterhead in a font called <span class="no-hyphens">Stencil:</span>

Who were his target clients? Army-surplus stores? He explained that he wanted something <span class="no-hyphens">distinctive.</span>

Distinctive is fine. Goofy is <span class="no-hyphens">not.</span>

From the top: no, no, no, no, and hell <span class="no-hyphens">no.</span>

Novelty fonts, script fonts, handwriting fonts, circus fonts—these have no place in any document created by a professional writer. Save them for your next career as a designer of breakfast-cereal <span class="no-hyphens">boxes.</span>

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- Don’t misunderstand—I completely believe in the power of a font to make an impression. Some of these fonts might be useful in a sign or a billboard, where the goal is to attract attention using limited space. But in a document that invites the patience and attention of a reader, a goofy font is as subtle as a jackhammer in a library. And equally <span class="no-hyphens">unwelcome.</span>

- If you think we live in the golden age of goofy fonts—not even close. Typefounders of the 19th century did it all (and more, and <span class="no-hyphens">better).</span>
