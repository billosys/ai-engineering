semicolons and colonsDon’t mix them up

The semicolon (;) has two primary <span class="no-hyphens">uses.</span>

1.  It’s used instead of a conjunction to combine two sentences (He did the crime; he must do the time). Don’t use a semicolon to connect a subordinate clause to a sentence (Since he did the crime; he must do the time). In that case, use a <span class="no-hyphens">comma.</span>

    Exceptions exist. For comprehensive explanations, consult Garner’s Modern English Usage (see <a href="bibliography.html" class="xref">bibliography</a>), the authority on how to use semicolons, colons, other punctuation marks, and the rest of the English <span class="no-hyphens">language.</span>

    In a sentence with a conjunction, don’t use a semicolon before the conjunction (He did the crime; and he must do the time). Use either a comma or no punctuation before the conjunction. Or start a new sentence at the <span class="no-hyphens">conjunction.</span>

2.  A semicolon also separates list elements with internal commas (*We visited Tulsa, Oklahoma; Flint, Michigan; and Paducah, Kentucky*).

The colon (:) usually connects the introduction of an idea and its completion (*I own three cars: a convertible, a sedan, and a minivan*).

Semicolons are often mistakenly used where a colon is correct, and vice versa. Make sure you’ve got the right <span class="no-hyphens">one.</span>
