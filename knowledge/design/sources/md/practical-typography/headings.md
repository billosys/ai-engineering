headingsFewer levels, subtler emphasis

Headings present two problems: structural and typographic. Cure the structural problem and the typographic problem becomes <span class="no-hyphens">simpler.</span>

The structural problem is that writers often use too many levels of headings. This leads to increasingly desperate attempts to make them visually distinct, usually with injudicious combinations of <a href="bold-or-italic.html" class="xref">bold or italic</a>, <a href="underlining.html" class="xref">underlining</a>, <a href="point-size.html" class="xref">point size</a>, <a href="all-caps.html" class="xref">all caps</a>, and <a href="first-line-indents.html" class="xref">first-line indents</a>. The result is trainwrecks like <span class="no-hyphens">this:</span>

5(a)(1)(iv) The Target Market  
Has Expressed a Preference for  
Copious Banner <span class="no-hyphens">Advertising.</span>

Headings are signposts for readers that reveal the structure of your argument. Note that I didn’t say the structure of your *document*. Headings that announce every topic, subtopic, minitopic, and microtopic are exhausting. If you write from an outline, that can be a good starting point for your headings, but don’t stop there—simplify it <span class="no-hyphens">further.</span>

Limit yourself to three levels of headings. Two is better. Readers should be able to orient themselves from the headings. With more than three levels, that task becomes hopelessly confusing. You may know your argument inside out, but no one else does (or <span class="no-hyphens">will).</span>

In journalism, *burying the lead* describes the error of putting the key idea of your story somewhere in the middle. Traditionally it was spelled *lede*, not *lead*, to distinguish it from the material used for casting metal <span class="no-hyphens">type.</span>

Once you’ve cured the structural problem, work within these typographic <span class="no-hyphens">parameters:</span>

1.  Don’t use <a href="all-caps.html" class="xref">all caps</a>. If your headings are full sentences, then they’re too long for caps. And Always Avoid Title Case, Because Your Headings Aren’t <span class="no-hyphens">Titles.</span>

2.  Don’t underline. Why? Review <a href="underlining.html" class="xref">underlining</a>.

3.  Don’t center, subject to the exceptions in <a href="centered-text.html" class="xref">centered text</a>.

4.  The best way to emphasize a heading is by putting <a href="space-above-and-below.html" class="xref">space above and below</a>, because it’s both subtle and <span class="no-hyphens">effective.</span>

5.  Use bold, not italic. For headings, bold is easier to read than italic and stands out better on the page. And since the choice is <a href="bold-or-italic.html" class="xref">bold or italic</a>—not both—you should prefer bold. But even then, it’s an option, not a requirement. Non-bold headings work <span class="no-hyphens">too.</span>

6.  It’s fine to make the <a href="point-size.html" class="xref">point size</a> bigger, but just a little. Use the smallest increment necessary to make a visible difference. If your text is set in 12 point, you needn’t go up to 14 or 15 point. Try a smaller increase—to 12.5 or 13 <span class="no-hyphens">point.</span>

7.  Only use two levels of indenting, even if you use more than two levels of headings. Some writers like to indent every heading a little farther. Bad idea. It ends up looking random and <span class="no-hyphens">messy.</span>

8.  Suppress <a href="hyphenation.html" class="xref">hyphenation</a> in headings, and use the <a href="keep-lines-together.html" class="xref">keep lines together</a> and <a href="keep-with-next-paragraph.html" class="xref">keep with next paragraph</a> options to prevent headings from breaking <span class="no-hyphens">awkwardly.</span>

9.  See <a href="hierarchical-headings.html" class="xref">hierarchical headings</a> for a contrarian suggestion about how to number <span class="no-hyphens">headings.</span>

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- If you’re using bold in your heading, you can also try *reducing* the point size by a half or full point. If your font has a relatively heavy bold style (like <a href="times-new-roman-alternatives.html" class="xref">Times New Roman</a>), reducing the size can offset the effect of the darker color, giving you subtler <span class="no-hyphens">emphasis.</span>

- Certain web-design pundits claim that [modular scale](https://alistapart.com/article/more-meaningful-typography)—that is, multiplying the <a href="body-text.html" class="xref">body text</a> by a recurring ratio—is a useful method of sizing web headings. I disagree. Yes, mathematical tools can guide certain typographic choices (see <a href="grids.html" class="xref">grids</a>, for instance). The risk with these shortcuts is that they encourage typographers to satisfy themselves with numerical justifications—*I used the golden ratio, therefore it must look good!*—at the expense of developing visual judgment. When your headings look right, they *are* right. The ratio is irrelevant. (FWIW, I’ve never used a modular scale to size type. And never <span class="no-hyphens">will.)</span>
