foot and inch marksUse straight quotes, not curly

Foot and inch marks—also known as minute and second marks or prime and double prime marks, depending on what they’re labeling—are not curly. Use straight quotes for these <span class="no-hyphens">marks.</span>

|     |           | Windows | Mac OS | HTML |
|-----|-----------|---------|--------|------|
| '   | foot mark | '       | '      | '    |
| "   | inch mark | "       | "      | "    |

“But back in <a href="straight-and-curly-quotes.html" class="xref">straight and curly quotes</a>, you told me to turn on smart quotes. So now, when I try to type foot and inch marks, they come out curly, not straight.” A fair <span class="no-hyphens">point.</span>

<div class="howto">

<div class="howto-name">

How to override smart quotes

</div>

WordType the foot or inch mark, which will come out curly, and then press the key command for Undo. The curly mark will become <span class="no-hyphens">straight.</span>

PagesSame as Word—type the foot or inch mark, which will come out curly, and then press ⌘ + Z for Undo.

HTMLUse the explicit code for the single straight quote (`&#39;`) and double straight quote (`&#34;` or `&quot;`)

</div>

The same symbols are used to denote arcminutes (= 1/60th of a radial degree) and arcseconds (= 1/60th of an arcminute). See <a href="screen-reading-considerations.html" class="xref">screen-reading considerations</a> for how the arcminute influences <span class="no-hyphens">typography.</span>

Typography purists would point out that proper foot and inch marks have a slight northeast-to-southwest slope to them. HTML purists would further point out that these characters have their own escape codes (`&prime;` and `&Prime;`). True, and you are welcome to seek them <span class="no-hyphens">out.</span>

|                 |        |
|-----------------|--------|
| –118° 19′ 36.9″ | purity |

What you will find is that even though these characters are available in <a href="system-fonts.html" class="xref">system fonts</a>, they are less common in professional fonts (found in <a href="font-recommendations.html" class="xref">font recommendations</a>). So as a rule, straight quotes are the most reliable tools for the <span class="no-hyphens">job.</span>

|                 |              |
|-----------------|--------------|
| –118° 19’ 36.9” | wrong        |
| –118° 19' 36.9" | close enough |

You can italicize the straight quotes if you want an even better approximation of sloped foot and inch <span class="no-hyphens">marks.</span>

|                     |              |
|---------------------|--------------|
| –118° 19*'* 36.9*"* | bonus points |

Tread carefully if foot and inch marks appear within quoted <span class="no-hyphens">material.</span>

|                 |          |
|-----------------|----------|
| “She’s 6’ 10”.” | wrong    |
| "She's 6’ 10”." | hopeless |
| “She’s 6' 10".” | right    |

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- Why would professional fonts omit these characters? Foot and inch marks have never had a direct key shortcut on either Mac or Windows, nor have they been part of any standard character-set specification, so they have gone obscure in the digital era. But they are starting to <span class="no-hyphens">reappear.</span>

- If you’re a purist whose chosen font is missing true foot and inch marks, there’s nothing wrong with using the marks from a different font. Match the height and weight as best you can. No one will <span class="no-hyphens">know.</span>

- A <a href="nonbreaking-spaces.html" class="xref">nonbreaking space</a> in the middle of a foot-and-inch measurement will prevent an awkward linebreak like *6'  
  10"*, and ensure that you get *6' 10"*.

</div>
