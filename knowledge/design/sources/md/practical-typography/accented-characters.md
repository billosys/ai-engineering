accented charactersDon’t ignore them

I assume you’re writing in American English, but you might still encounter accented characters in foreign words. Foreign words arise in two <span class="no-hyphens">situations:</span>

1.  As proper names, like people and places (Albrecht Dürer, François Truffaut, Plácido Domingo). In names, accented characters must always appear accurately. Otherwise, the name is <span class="no-hyphens">misspelled.</span>

2.  As loanwords used in American English. Some of these words have become naturalized citizens and should be spelled without accents (naive for naïve, melee for mêlée, coupe for coupé). Others have not and should not (cause célèbre, piña colada, Götterdämmerung). Check a dictionary or usage <span class="no-hyphens">guide.</span>

How do you type these? Consult the chart of <a href="common-accented-characters.html" class="xref">common accented characters</a>.

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- Proper names are not italicized, but loanwords sometimes are, depending on their degree of assimilation. Again, check a dictionary or usage <span class="no-hyphens">guide.</span>

- Ein vergrößertes <span class="no-hyphens">Eszett</span>

  The German letter Eszett (at left), formerly a special <a href="ligatures.html" class="xref">ligature</a>, in 2017 became a [full member](http://typedrawers.com/discussion/comment/28862/#Comment_28862) of the Standard German alphabet. Originally, it took the place of ss in a word like Straße. But in today’s Standard German, words like Masse and Maße are distinct. Switzerland, however, has always ignored the Eszett, and just uses ss. Your best bet—follow Switzerland’s lead and ignore it. Even if you place the Eszett correctly, it’s less common to readers of American English than the usual accented characters, and it can easily be mistaken for a letter B or a Greek beta (β).
