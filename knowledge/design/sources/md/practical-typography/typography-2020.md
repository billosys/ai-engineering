Typography 2020:  
A special listicle for America

“You cannot bore people into buying your product”, [according to David Ogilvy.](https://www.entrepreneur.com/article/197680) So true. Nevertheless, election season arrives, and radical boredom inevitably becomes the preferred strategy for most candidates. Let’s have a look at the typography <span class="no-hyphens">anyhow.</span>

<div id="the-gotham-administration" class="subhead">

[The Gotham administration](#the-gotham-administration)

</div>

Presidential-campaign typography took a big step up in 2008, when Barack Obama adopted the then-new [Gotham](https://www.typography.com/fonts/gotham/overview/) font [for his campaign.](https://web.archive.org/web/20080206151434/http://www.barackobama.com/index.php) (Though for his re-election campaign, he [had serifs added.](https://www.typography.com/blog/can-we-add-serifs-to-gotham%0A)) This led to the [rise of Gotham](http://gothamiseverywhere.tumblr.com/) throughout the United States. But especially in political campaigns, where the geometric sans has become typographic shorthand for `#winning`.

Gone but not forgotten <span class="no-hyphens"></span>

Interestingly, one of Obama’s few bipartisan successes was inducing Republicans to use Gotham too: in 2016, it was chosen by [Ted Cruz](https://blog.union.io/type/2016/02/16/presidential-font-reviews/) and Donald Trump (well, the no-cost Gotham knockoff [Montserrat](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Montserrat)).

Along those lines, should a Joe Biden campaign materialize, the presence of Gotham will be an indicator of how much he plans to run on Obama nostalgia. Anything but Gotham would be a more encouraging sign—someone who’s been around forever should find a way to surprise. Still, I’ll be surprised if I turn out to be surprised. \[Update: [Joe Biden](#joe-biden) has entered the race. Without <span class="no-hyphens">Gotham!\]</span>

<div id="the-democratic-field-as-of-31-january-2020" class="subhead">

[The Democratic field as of 31 January 2020](#the-democratic-field-as-of-31-january-2020)

</div>

I relied on the NYT to tell me [who’s running for <span class="no-hyphens">real.</span>](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2019/us/politics/2020-presidential-candidates.html)

I wasn’t impressed by any of the websites, none of which exceeded the high end of mediocre—what you might find in an [\$18/month Squarespace <span class="no-hyphens">plan.</span>](https://www.squarespace.com/pricing)

- Of course, there were a ton of Gothamesque geometric sans <span class="no-hyphens">serifs.</span>

- The candidate who was most successful stoking my curiosity with design was [Kirsten <span class="no-hyphens">Gillibrand.</span>](#kirsten-gillibrand)

- I was surprised that the long-shot candidates weren’t taking more chances with their websites—what the hell have they got to lose? Though I suppose [Julián Castro,](#julin-castro) whose site was as boring as any, was nevertheless the most <span class="no-hyphens">competent.</span>

- Among current front-runner-ish candidates, [Kamala Harris](#kamala-harris) was the worst underperformer, with [Beto O’Rourke](#beto-orourke) second <span class="no-hyphens">worst.</span>

- Overall best in show: I am surprised to say it’s [Joe <span class="no-hyphens">Biden.</span>](#joe-biden)

- Overall worst in show: [Cory Booker,](#cory-booker) who apparently decided to run for president on a Monday, crowdsourced his website on Tuesday, and launched it on Wednesday. <span class="no-hyphens">Unbearable.</span>

For those who think it trivializes our political process to judge candidates by their typography—what would you prefer we scrutinize? Qualifications? Ground into dust during the last election. Issues? Be my guest. Whether a candidate will ever fulfill a certain campaign promise about a certain issue is <span class="no-hyphens">conjectural.</span>

But typography—that’s a real decision candidates have to make today, with real money and real consequences. And if I can’t trust you to pick some reasonable fonts and colors, then why should I trust you with the nuclear <span class="no-hyphens">codes?</span>

The adage “don’t judge a book by its cover” dates from the time before books were sold with colorful <span class="no-hyphens">jackets.</span>

Book publishers spend a lot on cover design. Candidates likewise spend a lot on their public presentation. Why? For the same reasons: voters (or readers) are going to make judgments based on design factors (whether consciously or not). So just as we should feel justified judging a book by its cover—because that’s what it’s for—we should likewise feel justified considering how typography reflects on each <span class="no-hyphens">candidate.</span>

Along those lines, my pet political theory is that even as they labor to reveal their characteristic strengths through typography, candidates tend to be more successful revealing their characteristic limitations. The evidence is left as a thought experiment for sufficiently motivated <span class="no-hyphens">readers.</span>

<div id="michael-bennet" class="subhead">

[Michael Bennet](#michael-bennet)

</div>

[](https://michaelbennet.com/)

Now that Mighty [Joe Biden](#joe-biden) has entered the race, subsequent candidates are going to have to paddle harder to answer two critical questions: *Who are you?* and *Why are you here?* Bennet’s answer seems to be that he has the politics of fellow Coloradan [John Hickenlooper,](#john-hickenlooper) the web design of [Eric Swalwell,](#eric-swalwell) and in a weirdly flagrant pinch, the arched logo of [Pete Buttigieg](#pete-buttigieg) (combined with some Gotham “for America”). Text is [Source Serif;](https://fonts.adobe.com/fonts/source-serif) display is [Source Sans.](https://fonts.adobe.com/fonts/source-sans) Among free fonts, I’ve [recommended these,](free-fonts.md) but here, they contribute to an overall vibe of Bennet as the knockoff-brand <span class="no-hyphens">candidate.</span>

<div id="joe-biden" class="subhead">

[Joe Biden](#joe-biden)

</div>

[](https://joebiden.com/)

Argh, it would be more fun to hate this. Daring, it’s not. But to give Joe his due—it’s simple, direct, and well done. The design is meant to look relaxed and effortless, but accomplishes that only by being exceedingly specific in its details. For instance, here on the home page, Joe’s tie is coordinated to match the color of the typography. The generous use of white space on every page is both punchy and elegant. Like all the candidates, the font is Gotham-like (in this case, [Brother 1816](https://tipotype.com/brother-1816/)). The red-white-and-blue color scheme is also no surprise, though the use of red is restricted to the wordmark, the donate buttons, and a few other small UI elements. ([Tulsi Gabbard](#tulsi-gabbard) overdoes it.) The wordmark tries a little too hard for corporate-logo cleverness, but I like the boss move of putting “President” right under <span class="no-hyphens">“Biden”.</span>

<div id="michael-bloomberg" class="subhead">

[Michael Bloomberg](#michael-bloomberg)

</div>

[](https://www.mikebloomberg.com/)

If you thought that Michael Bloomberg has been secretly plotting to jump into this race for years—nah. The proof: everything about his campaign website is slapdash and derivative, like a student who saunters into the final exam 10 minutes before the end and hurriedly cribs what he can from nearby students. The “two blues, one red” color scheme has already been done to death this year (see [Steve Bullock](#steve-bullock) for the list). The headlines are [Avenir Next;](https://www.myfonts.com/fonts/linotype/avenir-next-pro/) the body text is good old Georgia. The top-heavy logo looks like it came from Fiverr. Also, [wasn’t this guy a Republican](https://www.politico.com/news/2019/11/26/bloomberg-republican-endorsement-2020-073807) five minutes <span class="no-hyphens">ago?</span>

<div id="pete-buttigieg" class="subhead">

[Pete Buttigieg](#pete-buttigieg)

</div>

[](https://peteforamerica.com/)

Note also the similar ETF and ETE trigrams. We report; you <span class="no-hyphens">decide.</span>

Pete has wisely avoided campaigning on his last name (though as a fellow Butt-prefixed American, I’m a little disappointed). The arched wordmark is a cute idea, though it appears stolen from [Netflix.](https://www.netflix.com/) Pete & chill? It could also have been rendered better—the capital Es are stretched awkwardly to fit the space. The splitting of 2020 around the mark is the kind of thing that makes people hate designers. Combined with the navy and gold colors, it’s reminiscent of a sports team, which is a body of typography I’m surprised candidates haven’t relied on more. Display font is [Aktiv Grotesk;](https://www.google.com/search?q=aktiv+grotesk&oq=aktiv+gro&aqs=chrome.0.0j69i57j0l4.2572j0j7&sourceid=chrome&ie=UTF-8) text is [Domaine.](https://klim.co.nz/retail-fonts/domaine-display/)

Disclosure: I donated to Pete based on his answer to a question about *Ulysses* and *Finnegans Wake* in a [recent interview.](https://www.esquire.com/news-politics/a26861236/peter-buttigieg-interview/) `#winning `<span class="no-hyphens">`#revengeofthenerds`</span>

<div id="tulsi-gabbard" class="subhead">

[Tulsi Gabbard](#tulsi-gabbard)

</div>

[](https://www.tulsi2020.com/)

I like the simplicity of the concept. The flat color field is arresting, even if it makes the candidate look like a flight attendant on Tulsi Airlines. I don’t like the take-perfectly-nice-font-and-chop-off-the-corners wordmark, a trend that [has been done to death](http://cuttingedgelogos.tumblr.com/) (and if you’re going to bother, why not chop off the L so it can sit closer to the S?) Text is [Neue Swift;](https://www.fontshop.com/families/neue-swift) display is [Harmonia <span class="no-hyphens">Sans.</span>](https://www.fontshop.com/families/harmonia-sans)

<div id="amy-klobuchar" class="subhead">

[Amy Klobuchar](#amy-klobuchar)

</div>

[](https://amyklobuchar.com/)

Amy—why are we on a first-name basis with our presidential candidates?—is the only candidate using traditional serifed lettering. Whoa! (I didn’t recognize the font, but internet sleuths tell me it’s [Mackay](https://www.renebieder.com/retail-fonts/mackay), which I now see is rather interesting, except for the three letters in “Amy”.) Like [Jay Inslee,](#jay-inslee) Amy’s mood ring is mostly blue and green. The layouts show a poor use of space, with areas that are largely empty alternating with areas tightly embroidered with text ([Julián Castro](#julin-castro) strikes a better balance). Text is the Gotham-like [Avenir Next.](https://www.fonts.com/font/linotype/avenir-next) Avenir is French for *future*—that almost qualifies as an [LTypI.](https://www.flickr.com/groups/ltypi/)

<div id="deval-patrick" class="subhead">

[Deval Patrick](#deval-patrick)

</div>

[](https://devalpatrick2020.com/)

As befits someone who announced his candidacy three seconds ago and hence has no donors, Patrick uses free fonts: the logo and headlines are [Barlow,](https://www.fontsquirrel.com/fonts/barlow) and body text is [Raleway.](https://www.fontsquirrel.com/fonts/raleway) Though watch your back, [Amy Klobuchar](#amy-klobuchar)—Patrick is apparently steering into the blue/green lane you’ve had to yourself thus far. The use of diagonal elements as a motif is plainly the decision of political neophytes who’ve not considered how many rectangular objects a campaign needs to <span class="no-hyphens">produce.</span>

<div id="bernie-sanders" class="subhead">

[Bernie Sanders](#bernie-sanders)

</div>

[](https://berniesanders.com)

I liked Bernie’s typography in 2016—his use of [Jubilat](https://www.dardenstudio.com/typefaces/jubilat) was unexpected but not discomfiting. Sort of like Bernie’s candidacy itself. He seems to be sticking with it for 2020, at the risk of creating a sense of “let’s do the same thing and expect different results!” (Even Obama, as an incumbent, [didn’t do that.](https://www.typography.com/blog/can-we-add-serifs-to-gotham%0A)) Text is [Gibson.](https://canadatype.com/product/gibson/) The background is not solid blue—there’s an autoplay video, but like many Americans, I block such <span class="no-hyphens">things.</span>

Disclosure: I donated to Bernie in <span class="no-hyphens">2016.</span>

<div id="tom-steyer" class="subhead">

[Tom Steyer](#tom-steyer)

</div>

[](https://tomsteyer.com/)

That’s *Steyer*, spelled with a *why*. Despite his late entry to the race, Steyer didn’t even have time for an irony review: apparently he wants to get [“corporate money out of politics”](https://www.tomsteyer.com/policies/) by first putting many of his own hedge-fund millions [into politics.](https://www.politico.com/news/2019/11/07/steyer-aide-offered-money-for-endorsements-067339) At least he bought a decent if samey font, which is [Centra No. 2.](https://sharptype.co/typefaces/centra/centra-no2/) The best feature of Steyer’s campaign is his pet pug, who is definitely a good boy, and probably a multimillionaire in his own <span class="no-hyphens">right.</span>

<div id="elizabeth-warren" class="subhead">

[Elizabeth Warren](#elizabeth-warren)

</div>

[](https://elizabethwarren.com/)

Some good ideas here—a few pay off, most don’t. I like the idea of a candidate’s website as being like an issue of the *New York Times* magazine—even the “Warren” wordmark makes it look like a new Condé Nast monthly. The fonts are smart ([Ringside](https://www.typography.com/fonts/ringside/overview/)) and the colors restrained (for a change). But as you click around it starts to look more similar to other candidates, especially [Kamala <span class="no-hyphens">Harris.</span>](#kamala-harris)

Disclosure: I donated to **WARREN**.

<div id="andrew-yang" class="subhead">

[Andrew Yang](#andrew-yang)

</div>

[](https://www.yang2020.com/)

Free advice for those with zero name recognition: don’t design a wordmark (in [Halyard,](http://halyard.dardenstudio.com) FWIW) that makes it hard to read your name. The Y in Yang looks like—what, a slice of bacon using a playground slide in an unsafe way? Everything about this website confuses me. I can’t tell if Andrew Yang thinks that’s a feature or a bug. Like [Amy Klobuchar,](#amy-klobuchar) Yang also discovered the oh-so-appropriately-named [Avenir](https://www.fonts.com/font/linotype/avenir-next) for <span class="no-hyphens">text.</span>

[Dropouts: gone but not forgotten](#dropouts-gone-but-not-forgotten)

</div>

<div id="eric-swalwell" class="subhead">

[Eric Swalwell](#eric-swalwell)

</div>

[](https://ericswalwell.com/)

I compliment the wordmark designer for not fumbling the difficult spacing around the two Ws (which would be an ongoing challenge of a Swalwell presidency). Display is [Industry Inc,](https://fortfoundry.com/pages/industry-inc) text is the Gothamesque [Effra.](https://www.daltonmaag.com/library/effra) Colors are red, white, and ... you know. But Swallwell’s website shouts platitudes like “go big” and “be bold” even though he’s doing neither with his typography. Maybe check out [Kirsten Gillibrand,](#kirsten-gillibrand) who, on this issue, walks the <span class="no-hyphens">walk.</span>

(Dropped out in July <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="john-hickenlooper" class="subhead">

[John Hickenlooper](#john-hickenlooper)

</div>

[](https://www.hickenlooper.com/)

It could be worse. But it still looks more like he’s starting an outdoor-clothing label, not running for president. Like other candidates, Hickenlooper evokes Obama’s use of Gotham (with the similar [Proxima Nova,](https://www.marksimonson.com/fonts/view/proxima-nova) though Proxima predates Gotham). The deep purple is unexpected—Colorado is politically “purple”, is that the idea?—though pairing two intense colors doesn’t provide much versatility ([Marianne Williamson](#marianne-williamson) did better). Hickenlooper would’ve done better to emulate the clever economy of the Colorado license plate, where one color serves as both foreground and <span class="no-hyphens">background.</span>

(Dropped out in August <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="jay-inslee" class="subhead">

[Jay Inslee](#jay-inslee)

</div>

[](https://www.jayinslee.com/)

The typography makes it look like a pharmaceutical ad—*Ask your doctor if Inslee™ is right for you.* Jay Inslee wants to give you lots of colors: American red, white, and blue, and a lighter blue, and then a couple shades of green, I suppose to connote that he cares about the environment. Another candidate evoking Obama’s Gotham typography (this time with [Montserrat](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Montserrat)).

(Dropped out in August <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="seth-moulton" class="subhead">

[Seth Moulton](#seth-moulton)

</div>

[](https://sethmoulton.com/)

As with fellow late entrant [Michael Bennet,](#michael-bennet) Seth Moulton seems like déjà vu all over again. Two shades of blue and one shade of red? Check. Free fonts? Check—[League Gothic](https://www.theleagueofmoveabletype.com/league-gothic) for display and [Metropolis](https://github.com/chrismsimpson/Metropolis) for text. Wait, does one of them look like Gotham? Check. Is he “for America”? Check. Moulton is likely to spend most of his short campaign explaining that no, he’s not [Pete Buttigieg.](#pete-buttigieg) As for that logo: did no one consider the awkward symbolism of the right-pointing red <span class="no-hyphens">arrow?</span>

(Dropped out in August <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="kirsten-gillibrand" class="subhead">

[Kirsten Gillibrand](#kirsten-gillibrand)

</div>

[](https://kirstengillibrand.com/)

I mostly like this—simple and punchy—with the caveat that the hot-pink color scheme reminded me of a [cancer-awareness nonprofit.](https://ww5.komen.org/) But Gillibrand’s slogan is “brave wins”. The design choices are likewise brave (well, for a politician—it’s a low bar). Display fonts are [Navigo](https://type.today/en/navigo) and [Alternate Gothic;](https://fonts.adobe.com/fonts/alternate-gothic) text is [Libre Franklin](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Libre+Franklin) (though did she hear about [Public Sans?](https://tinyletter.com/mbutterick/letters/the-curious-case-of-public-sans-billionaire-s-typewriter-update))

(Dropped out in August <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="bill-de-blasio" class="subhead">

[Bill de Blasio](#bill-de-blasio)

</div>

[](https://billdeblasio.com/)

The good news: a genuinely novel display face (the two-tone [Acier](https://www.productiontype.com/family/acier)) and a solid sans for text ([Gibson](https://canadatype.com/product/gibson/%0A), also used by [Bernie Sanders](#bernie-sanders)). The bad news: everything else. The horrific colors make “de Blasio” look like a [floor-cleaning brand.](https://swiffer.com/en-us) The logo comes from the [Wayne Messam](#wayne-messam) school of typographic misproportion. The whole thing is crude, amateurish, and inexplicable. The only thing that prevents it from displacing [Cory Booker](#cory-booker) in last place is that there isn’t much of <span class="no-hyphens">it.</span>

(Dropped out in September <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="tim-ryan" class="subhead">

[Tim Ryan](#tim-ryan)

</div>

[](https://timryanforamerica.com/)

Tim Ryan seems to have gotten his logo and website from the same factory-outlet store as [Cory Booker.](#cory-booker) Ryan has slightly better production values, but seems to consist mostly of photos and videos taken on smartphones. Fonts are nothing special: the freely available [Open Sans](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Open+Sans) and [Poppins.](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Poppins)

(Dropped out in October <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="beto-orourke" class="subhead">

[Beto O’Rourke](#beto-orourke)

</div>

[](https://betoorourke.com/)

After the Playskool color schemes of the other candidates, Beto O’Rourke’s monochromatic wordmark and website are a good start. Things go downhill from there, however. The website seems like it could’ve been made 15 years ago, with its menus of tiny type linking to pages filled with more tiny type. Display is [Prohibition](https://fortfoundry.com/pages/prohibition) and [URW DIN;](https://fonts.adobe.com/fonts/urw-din) text is [Tisa.](https://www.fontshop.com/families/ff-tisa)

(Dropped out in November <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="wayne-messam" class="subhead">

[Wayne Messam](#wayne-messam)

</div>

[](https://wayneforamerica.com/)

Like [Jay Inslee](#jay-inslee), Messam’s website also uses a red–green–blue color scheme with a geometric Gotham-style sans ([Montserrat](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Montserrat), also favored by the current commander-in-chief). The wordmark is wildly misproportioned, with “Wayne” in danger of crushing “America”. If the WA is going to be kerned so tightly, it calls out for a <a href="ligatures.html" class="xref">ligature</a>—maybe speak to whoever did the wordmark for [Eric Swalwell,](#eric-swalwell) who clearly has a specialty in the capital W. Unclear why Messam thinks it’s wise to feature a photo of himself running away from the <span class="no-hyphens">voter.</span>

(Dropped out in November <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="joe-sestak" class="subhead">

[Joe Sestak](#joe-sestak)

</div>

[](https://joesestak.com/)

When I first published this piece, I said I was surprised that no candidate had ever used [Interstate,](https://fonts.adobe.com/fonts/interstate) which is modeled on the U.S. highway font. In an amazing coincidence, very late entrant Joe Sestak has launched his campaign using [Overpass,](https://www.fontsquirrel.com/fonts/overpass) an open-source family based on the same model. Am I the only one who thinks Sestak looks like he could be [Pete Buttigieg’s](#pete-buttigieg) dad? Other than that, the only place where Sestak is competitive is the race for most peculiar logo, where he might edge out [Andrew Yang](#andrew-yang) and [Seth Moulton.](#seth-moulton) Sestak’s eyeball-shaped logo looks like it was made with Corel Draw in 1997. Apparently no one noticed that his slogan “Accountability to America” is accompanied by a drawing of North America, Central America, and South America. Is he running for President of the <span class="no-hyphens">Hemisphere?</span>

(Dropped out in December <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="steve-bullock" class="subhead">

[Steve Bullock](#steve-bullock)

</div>

[](https://stevebullock.com/)

Late entrant Bullock brings something truly new to the campaign season: slab serifs in his logo and headlines ([FF Kievit Slab](https://www.fontshop.com/families/ff-kievit-slab)) and a wide sans ([Ringside,](https://www.typography.com/fonts/ringside/overview) a font family also used by [Elizabeth Warren](#elizabeth-warren)). Both nice fonts. Text is the unremarkable [Open Sans.](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Open+Sans) Otherwise not a lot to distinguish Bullock’s presence—pretty much the same “two blues, one red” color scheme of [Michael Bennet,](#michael-bennet) [Joe Biden,](#joe-biden) [Seth Moulton,](#seth-moulton) [Eric Swallwell](#eric-swallwell), and [Andrew Yang](#andrew-yang), and a similar layout too. I appreciate, however, that one of the headlines on the home page is “Who the heck is Steve <span class="no-hyphens">Bullock?”</span>

(Dropped out in December <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="kamala-harris" class="subhead">

[Kamala Harris](#kamala-harris)

</div>

[](https://kamalaharris.org/)

I like the display type a lot ([Bureau Grotesque](https://fonts.adobe.com/fonts/bureau-grot)). But lordy, the colors are nauseating. Apparently Kamala intended to [pay tribute](https://theslot.jezebel.com/kamala-harris-pays-homage-to-shirley-chisholm-in-her-pr-1831925814) to the red and yellow colors of 1972 candidate [Shirley Chisholm.](https://www.womenshistory.org/education-resources/biographies/shirley-chisholm) Fair enough, but something got lost in translation: the red became reddish orange, the intense yellow became goldenrod, and then she threw in bluish purple too. Here in California, the knock on Kamala is that she’s always been eager to be seen as all things to all people. “What color do you want in a candidate’s logo? Well I’ve got that <span class="no-hyphens">color!”</span>

(Dropped out in December <span class="no-hyphens">2019.)</span>

<div id="julin-castro" class="subhead">

[Julián Castro](#julin-castro)

</div>

[](https://www.julianforthefuture.com/)

As a layout, not inventive—looks like a middling WordPress theme. But clever in places: for instance, the colorized accent over Á in the wordmark. Also the font choice, which alludes to Gotham by using [Mallory,](https://frerejones.com/families/mallory) another geometric sans by the same designer, Tobias Frere-Jones. Castro finds a nice balance of headlines that aren’t too big and <a href="body-text.html" class="xref">body text</a> that isn’t too small, with ample white space too. In contrast to his competitors, most of whom favor acres of tiny type, like they were designing for the web of 1996. Text is [Fedra.](https://www.typotheque.com/fonts/fedra_serif_a)

(Dropped out in January <span class="no-hyphens">2020.)</span>

<div id="marianne-williamson" class="subhead">

[Marianne Williamson](#marianne-williamson)

</div>

[](https://www.marianne2020.com/)

I like Williamson’s pale pink & indigo color scheme. The arty illustration is distinctive, if odd. The fonts are not terrible ([Cormorant Garamond](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Cormorant+Garamond) and [Poppins](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/Poppins)) but the actual layouts are sloppy and indifferent. Still, more than I was expecting from a looooong-shot <span class="no-hyphens">campaign.</span>

(Dropped out in January <span class="no-hyphens">2020.)</span>

<div id="cory-booker" class="subhead">

[Cory Booker](#cory-booker)

</div>

[](https://corybooker.com/)

The weird layout in the screenshot is exactly as I found it, and the popups refused to be dismissed. The display font [Conductor](https://frerejones.com/families/conductor) has potential. But there’s no design concept to speak of (no, “red, white, and blue” doesn’t cut it). The execution is totally inept. Cory, it was nice of you to hire your second cousin. But seek professional help. You’re trailing the <span class="no-hyphens">pack.</span>

(Dropped out in January <span class="no-hyphens">2020.)</span>

<div id="john-delaney" class="subhead">

[John Delaney](#john-delaney)

</div>

[](https://www.johndelaney.com/)

Looks like an early draft of the [Julián Castro](#julin-castro) website. Another geometric sans serif for display ([Avant Garde](https://fontsinuse.com/uses/21625/avant-garde-magazine-13)) though the sizing, spacing, and alignment is wrong at every turn. For someone with a relatively low profile, Delaney is missing an opportunity to distinguish himself with typography. Confidential to muscular bald dudes: posing with crossed arms may remind some of [Mr. <span class="no-hyphens">Clean.</span>](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mr._Clean)

(Dropped out in January <span class="no-hyphens">2020.)</span>

[Message: I care](#message-i-care)

by the way

- One font I’m very surprised has never shown up in a presidential campaign: [Interstate,](https://fonts.adobe.com/fonts/interstate) which is modeled on the U.S. highway font, the one typeface that every American voter is already familiar with. (Update: [Joe Sestak](#joe-sestak) took my <span class="no-hyphens">hint.)</span>

- The big problem with red & blue as colors is that they look great with white, but terrible with each other, because they have weak contrast, creating a vibrating op-art effect. The photo at left shows how the red tends to disappear into the blue, making the logo look like **BID N**. The designers of the flags for America, Great Britain, France, Norway, Costa Rica, Dominican Republic, and North Korea figured this out a long time <span class="no-hyphens">ago.</span>
