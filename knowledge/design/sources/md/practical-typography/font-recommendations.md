Font recommendations

Chromatic wood type specimen, early 1900s. This page would’ve needed eight passes through the <span class="no-hyphens">press.</span>

Fonts are only one ingredient of typography. And messing around with the font menu on your computer isn’t a substitute for knowing the fundamentals of <a href="type-composition.html" class="xref">type composition</a> and <a href="text-formatting.html" class="xref">text formatting</a>. That’s why this chapter appears in the middle of the book, not the <span class="no-hyphens">beginning.</span>

But we’ve covered that, right? So here’s the secret sauce: If you want the fastest, easiest and most obvious upgrade to your typography, nothing beats a professional <span class="no-hyphens">font.</span>

As a designer of professional fonts—including the ones used in this book—am I biased? Of course. But no one has seriously disputed that it’s <span class="no-hyphens">true.</span>

If you consider the alternatives in this chapter and still prefer <a href="times-new-roman-alternatives.html" class="xref">Times New Roman</a> or other <a href="system-fonts.html" class="xref">system fonts</a>, I won’t think less of you. I’ll even concede that there are situations, like <a href="emails.html" class="xref">emails</a> and draft documents, where system fonts are your best <span class="no-hyphens">option.</span>

But in general, for writers who care about typography, professional fonts are essential tools.  <span class="no-hyphens"></span>
