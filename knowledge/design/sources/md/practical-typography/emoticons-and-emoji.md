emoticons & emojiOK in email or texts, not in formal writing

*Emoticons* are typographic illustrations, usually made of punctuation characters, intended as shorthand for certain authorial intent that might not otherwise be apparent in a short digital communication. [Since 1982,](http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~sef/Orig-Smiley.htm) they’ve been part of the internet (when that word was still <span class="no-hyphens">capitalized).</span>

Latterly they’ve been displaced by *emoji*, which are single pictorial characters that accomplish the same thing. Indeed, emoji are basically <a href="ligatures.html" class="xref">ligatures</a> for <span class="no-hyphens">emoticons.</span>

Still, emoticon innovation continues, most prominently *kaomoji*, which are [emoticons that use longer combinations](https://slangit.com/emoticons/kaomoji) of Japanese and other non-Latin characters. Most <span class="no-hyphens">famously—</span>

¯\\(ツ)\_/¯

As a typographer and writer, I avoided emoticons for many years, thinking them jejune. But as I found myself needing to correspond with more strangers, I grudgingly acceded. My go-to emoticon—the short winker `;)`—has helped prevent my [attempts at dry humor](https://www.mcsweeneys.net/articles/the-review-submission-policies-agreement-form) from being misinterpreted. Furthermore, Bryan Garner (see <a href="bibliography.html" class="xref">bibliography</a>) [endorses emoticons.](https://twitter.com/search?q=%3A-)%20from%3Abryanagarner&src=typd&lang=en) So I’m persuaded that they’re not contributing to the decline of world culture. Much, <span class="no-hyphens">anyhow.</span>

Three caveats, <span class="no-hyphens">however—</span>

1.  Emoticons and emoji, even more so than <a href="ampersands.html" class="xref">ampersands</a>, are casual shorthand. So in non-casual settings—for instance, on your <a href="resumes.html" class="xref">résumé</a>—don’t.

2.  Overuse of these symbols is just as irritating as the overuse of <a href="question-marks-and-exclamation-points.html" class="xref">question marks and exclamation points</a>, or <a href="all-caps.html" class="xref">all caps</a>. If you find yourself punctuating every sentence with `:-)` to avoid misunderstandings, learn to write <span class="no-hyphens">better.</span>

3.  Though emoticons look mostly the same everywhere, emoji can [render differently](https://motherboard.vice.com/en_us/article/78kzn9/what-the-emoji-youre-sending-actually-look-like-to-your-friends) among [devices and platforms.](http://unicode.org/emoji/charts/full-emoji-list.html) So don’t count on <span class="no-hyphens">nuance.</span>

<div class="btw">

<div class="btw-title">

by the way

</div>

- Sideways emoticons should match the orientation of your writing system. So in English, a smiley is `:-)`, not `(-:`

- In 2018, an [iPhone bug](https://fuckyeahtypefacedesign.tumblr.com/post/171489245922) was discovered where simply sending the Telugu character జ్ఞా in a text message would crash the <span class="no-hyphens">phone.</span>
