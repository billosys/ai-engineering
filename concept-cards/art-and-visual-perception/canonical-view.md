# Quick Definition

The canonical view of an object is the projection angle that most directly and completely conveys the structural skeleton of the object's visual concept — the "best" view for recognition and representation.

# Core Definition

Because a 3D object can be projected from infinitely many angles, and no single 2D projection can capture all structural properties, the question arises: which view to choose?

Arnheim identifies two criteria for a "best" view:
1. **Simplicity**: Among all projections, those that produce the simplest, most regular patterns are perceptually dominant. "The dominant projections are those which produce patterns of the simplest shape." (p. 75)
2. **Structural adequacy**: The chosen view should make the structural skeleton of the visual concept directly visible. The projection's structural skeleton should reflect — not contradict — the structure of the object itself.

These criteria sometimes conflict. A straight front view of a horse (Figure 88) is the simplest projection but is inadequate because its structural skeleton (a compact, penguin-like outline) does not reflect the horse's actual structure. A side view is more complex as a projection but structurally more adequate. Similarly, a top-down view of a Mexican in a large hat (Figure 82) is a perfectly valid orthogonal projection but communicates nothing distinguishing about the subject — the projection's skeleton (a disk) misleads.

For objects with multiple parts whose best views differ, the artist must either choose a compromise view or use the Egyptian method of combining multiple best views.

# Prerequisites

- **Picture plane projection** — Canonical view is about which projection to select.
- **Structural skeleton** — The chosen view is "best" when its skeleton matches the object's skeleton.

# Key Properties

1. **Simplicity criterion**: All else equal, the view producing the simplest projection is preferred.
2. **Structural adequacy criterion**: The projection must make the structural skeleton of the visual concept visible — it must not mislead about the structure.
3. **Part-by-part conflict**: Different parts of a complex object often have different canonical views — requiring compositional choices.
4. **Early art preference**: Early (child and "primitive") art consistently uses canonical views — the most structurally informative aspect of each part.
5. **Artistic exploitation**: Atypical views can be used deliberately for expressive or humorous purposes precisely because they violate canonical view expectations.

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct/Create:
1. Form the visual concept of the object (comprehensive, all-around understanding of its structure).
2. Identify the structural properties that must be communicated (symmetry axes, characteristic proportions, defining features).
3. Select the projection angle(s) that best reveal those properties with maximum simplicity.
4. For objects with conflicting part-wise canonical views: decide whether to use the Egyptian method (combining views) or accept the trade-offs of a compromise single viewpoint.

## To Identify/Recognise:
1. Ask: does this view reveal the structural skeleton of the object? Or does it hide or misrepresent it?
2. Notice when an image uses atypical views for expressive effect — this is meaningful precisely against the baseline of the canonical view.
3. In icon design: check whether the chosen angle is the most recognisable one for the intended object type.

# Context & Application

- **Typical contexts**: Icon and pictogram design; product photography; information illustration; UX design (menu icons, toolbar buttons); scientific illustration.
- **Common applications**: Selecting the right view for an icon (a telephone icon is usually shown at an angle, not straight-on; a coffee mug from a slightly elevated side view); product photography choices; medical illustration.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 75, Fig. 82): A top-down view of a Mexican in a large sombrero is geometrically correct (an orthogonal projection) but is inadequate as a canonical view — the structure is indistinguishable from a millstone or a doughnut. The view fails the structural adequacy criterion.

**Example 2** (p. 75): A bull's typical shape is conveyed by a side view, which however hides the characteristic lyre pattern of the horns. The wingspread of a flying duck does not show in profile. Each part has a different canonical view.

**Example 3** (p. 76): The Egyptian method resolves the canonical-view problem by combining the most informative aspect of each part: front view of the eye in a profile head; front view of the chest with side view of the body. Each part is shown in its canonical view, even at the cost of projective consistency.

# Relationships

## Builds Upon
- **Picture plane projection** — Canonical view is the optimal selection among possible projections.
- **Structural skeleton** — The canonical view is the one that best reveals the structural skeleton.

## Enables
- **Representation in art** — Knowing the canonical view clarifies what the artist must accomplish.
- **Icon and pictogram design** — Canonical view selection is the primary design decision for recognisable icons.

## Related
- **Perceptual simplicity** — One criterion for canonical view is simplicity of the resulting projection.
- **Form vs. shape** — The canonical view is chosen to communicate form (structural meaning), not merely shape (geometric outline).

## Contrasts With
- **Atypical view** — Deliberately non-canonical views used for expressive, humorous, or dynamic purposes.
- **Mechanical projection** — Any fixed camera position produces a specific projection; canonical view requires selecting that position based on structural criteria.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming the most photographically natural or "realistic" view is canonical.
  **Correction**: The canonical view is determined by structural adequacy and simplicity, not photographic convention. The most "natural" angle for a camera may not be the most structurally informative view.

- **Error**: Designing all icons at the same angle (e.g., all at 45° isometric).
  **Correction**: Each object has its own canonical view determined by its specific structural properties. A uniform angle across all icons may serve stylistic consistency but can sacrifice structural legibility for some objects.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: "Canonical view" sounds like it means a standard or conventional angle.
  **Clarification**: While canonical views often become conventional (because they work), they are defined by perceptual and structural criteria, not by convention. The convention arises *from* the perceptual preference.
